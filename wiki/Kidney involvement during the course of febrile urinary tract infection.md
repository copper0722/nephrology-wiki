# Febrile Urinary Tract Infection (fUTI) and Renal Parenchymal Damage

## Exam Logic
The core of this topic lies in distinguishing between a simple lower UTI (cystitis) and **acute pyelonephritis** (fUTI), and understanding the subsequent risk of **permanent renal scarring**. 

*   **The "Gold Standard" Trap:** In exams, **DMSA (Dimercaptosuccinic acid) scanning** is the gold standard for detecting acute parenchymal involvement and chronic scarring. However, the "clinical pitfall" is that it is **not** used routinely due to radiation; diagnosis is primarily clinical/biochemical.
*   **Biomarker Specificity:** Not all inflammatory markers are equal. **Low CRP** is highly useful for *ruling out* pyelonephritis, while **Procalcitonin (PCT)** is more specific for *confirming* it. ESR is generally useless for this differentiation.
*   **Pathophysiology Paradox:** The innate immune response (specifically **PMN-derived ROS** and **M2 macrophage-mediated TGF-β1**) is necessary to clear UPEC but is the primary driver of **interstitial fibrosis** and CKD.
*   **High-Risk Cohorts:** Patients with **CAKUT** (Congenital Anomalies of the Kidney and Urinary Tract) are at significantly higher risk for fUTI and subsequent CKD due to underlying dysplasia and obstructive uropathy.

## Pathophysiology & Virulence
### Bacterial Mechanisms (UPEC)
*   **Adhesion:** **P fimbriae** (epithelial binding) and **Type 1 fimbriae** (internalization and biofilm formation).
*   **Cytotoxicity:** **$\alpha$-hemolysin (Hly)** $\rightarrow$ lyses erythrocytes (iron acquisition), activates **NF-κB**, and triggers the **NLRP3 inflammasome** $\rightarrow$ macrophage **pyroptosis**.
*   **Immune Evasion:** **TcpC** protein interferes with TLR signaling and suppresses ROS production to protect the bacteria.

### Host Response
1.  **Early Phase:** Clot formation in the proximal tubule (localized ischemia) to trap bacteria.
2.  **Inflammatory Cascade:** **TLR4** recognizes LPS $\rightarrow$ release of **IL-6, TNF, and IL-8**.
3.  **Cellular Infiltration:** 
    *   **PMNs:** First responders; use **ROS** to kill bacteria $\rightarrow$ causes collateral tissue damage.
    *   **M1 Macrophages:** Pro-inflammatory/phagocytic.
    *   **M2 Macrophages:** Resolution phase; secrete **MMP9** and **TGF-β1** $\rightarrow$ triggers myofibroblasts $\rightarrow$ **Renal Fibrosis**.
4.  **Antimicrobial Peptides:** **Tamm-Horsfall protein (THP)**, defensins, and cathelicidins. Note: Excessive THP can exacerbate interstitial nephritis.

## Clinical Presentation & Diagnosis
*   **Presentation:** Fever $\geq 38^\circ\text{C}$, malaise. In infants $< 3$ months, fever may be absent; look for irritability, vomiting, and lethargy (higher risk of sepsis/meningitis).
*   **Diagnostics:**
    *   **Urinalysis:** Leucocytes, nitrites.
    *   **Culture:** Gold standard for confirmation (single bacterial species).
    *   **Imaging:** DMSA scan (detects $\approx 85\%$ of parenchymal involvement).
*   **Prognosis:** 10–40% of fUTIs result in permanent scarring. Focal scarring usually does not cause severe GFR drop, but diffuse dysplasia (CAKUT) is a major risk for progressive CKD.
