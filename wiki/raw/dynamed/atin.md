# Acute Tubulointerstitial Nephritis (DynaMed)

## Definition
- Immune-mediated tubulointerstitial injury primarily triggered by medications, infections, autoimmune diseases
- Characterized by interstitial inflammatory infiltrate + tubulitis on histopathology
- 2-5% of all renal biopsies; 10-30% when biopsy done in AKI setting
- Drug-induced: 60-70% of cases; beta-lactams, NSAIDs, PPIs most common

## Types
- Drug-induced ATIN (most common): hypersensitivity features may be present with antibiotics but rare with NSAIDs/PPIs
- DRESS syndrome: very common hypersensitivity, latency 3-8 weeks, severe skin + multiorgan
- Infectious ATIN: no hypersensitivity features; high-grade fever
- Sarcoidosis-related ATIN: no typical hypersensitivity; may have hypercalcemia, pulmonary disease
- IgG4-related disease: storiform fibrosis, IgG4+ plasmablasts; pancreatitis, retroperitoneal fibrosis
- TINU (tubulointerstitial nephritis + uveitis): median age 15, F:M 3:1, 88% bilateral uveitis
- IgM-positive plasma cell TIN: distal RTA, Fanconi syndrome, high serum IgM

## Drug Causes
- Antibiotics: beta-lactams (penicillins, cephalosporins), sulfonamides, rifampin, fluoroquinolones, vancomycin
- Antivirals: acyclovir, antiretrovirals
- Anti-inflammatory: NSAIDs (all types), COX-2 inhibitors
- PPIs (omeprazole, lansoprazole) > H2 antagonists
- Immune checkpoint inhibitors (pembrolizumab, nivolumab, ipilimumab): onset 1-8 months
- Antiseizure: phenytoin, carbamazepine, phenobarbital
- Allopurinol
- Diuretics: furosemide, thiazides

## Clinical Presentation
- Classic triad (rash, fever, eosinophilia) only in 5-10% of cases
- Nonspecific symptoms: malaise, fatigue, anorexia, arthralgias (45%), fever (15-36%)
- Skin rash: 22-27% (morbilliform, trunk); mostly with beta-lactams/sulfonamides
- Lab: AKI, eosinophilia (23-36%), anemia, elevated ESR/CRP, elevated LFTs (if drug-induced hepatitis)
- Serum: hyperkalemia + hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis disproportionate to AKI degree → suspect ATIN
- Oliguria in ~50%

## Urine Findings
- Proteinuria: usually mild (< 1 g/day), nonnephrotic, tubular pattern
- Hematuria: microscopic in ~50% (close to 90% on beta-lactams)
- WBC/leukocytes: 50-80%; leukocyte esterase on dipstick
- WBC casts (3-14%), RBC casts (up to 29%), RTE cell casts + granular casts (up to 86%)
- Bland sediment in ~20%
- Eosinophiluria: historically considered useful but POOR sensitivity (19.8%) and specificity (91.2%) at >= 5% cutoff (CJASN 2013); PPV only 30% → NOT reliable for diagnosis

## Differentiating ATIN from ATN by Urine
- ATIN: WBC predominant, WBC casts, leukocyte esterase +, RTE/granular casts minimal
- ATN: RTE cells + muddy brown granular casts predominant, no WBCs

## Diagnosis
- Kidney biopsy required for definitive diagnosis
- Indicated if: diagnosis unclear, no improvement after stopping suspected drug, no contraindications
- May defer if patient improves after drug withdrawal
- Histology: interstitial inflammatory infiltrate (T lymphocytes + monocytes predominant) + tubulitis; eosinophils in drug-induced; granulomas in sarcoid/TINU/infections; IgG4+ plasma cells in IgG4-RD

## Prediction Rule (NDT 2022)
- EHR-based model: SCr, BUN:Cr ratio, urine specific gravity, urine dipstick proteinuria <= 1+
- c-statistic 0.74 in validation cohort (n = 1,118)

## Management
- Drug-induced: STOP offending medication immediately
- Corticosteroids: consider early (within 7 days) if no improvement after drug withdrawal
  - Prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day tapered over 3-4 weeks, with or without initial methylprednisolone pulse 250-500 mg/day x 2-4 days
  - Early steroids (within 7 days): complete recovery in 60% vs delayed > 7 days: higher risk incomplete recovery (OR 6.6)
  - Delayed steroids associated with interstitial fibrosis on biopsy (OR 8.68) and higher final SCr
- DRESS: stop drug + steroids + supportive
- Infectious: treat infection; additional medications based on course
- IgG4-related: steroids + consider steroid-sparing agents
- TINU: steroids for both renal and ocular components
- Steroid-resistant: isolated reports with MMF, antithymocyte globulin

## Prognosis
- Drug-induced with early treatment: complete recovery 41%, partial 46%, no recovery 13%
- Interstitial fibrosis on biopsy → poor prognostic sign (HR 5.55 for decreased renal recovery)
- 19% may require acute hemodialysis
