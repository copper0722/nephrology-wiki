# Rhabdomyolysis (DynaMed)

## Definition
- Skeletal muscle breakdown with release of intracellular contents (myoglobin, CK, aldolase, LDH, electrolytes) into circulation
- Classic triad: muscle pain, weakness/swelling, tea-colored urine (myoglobinuria)
- Diagnostic standard: elevated serum CK (typically 500-1,000 U/L; some use > 5x ULN)
- Myoglobin: short half-life → may be false negative; CK is more sensitive

## Epidemiology
- More common in males, Black persons, children < 10 or adults > 60, BMI > 40
- Exertional: 26.8/100,000 military recruits/year
- Traumatic injury: 85% of trauma patients
- Statin-induced: 0.1-8.4/100,000 persons/year

## Causes
- Trauma/crush injury (most common overall)
- Ischemia: thromboembolism, tourniquet, compartment syndrome
- Excessive muscle activity: seizures (especially status epilepticus), extreme exercise, delirium tremens
- Hyperthermia: heatstroke, malignant hyperthermia, NMS
- Hypothermia, near-drowning
- Electrical injury, lightning
- Medications: statins (especially with CYP3A4 inhibitors), fibrates, antipsychotics, lithium, colchicine, propofol, succinylcholine, daptomycin
  - Statin + clarithromycin/erythromycin: RR 2.17 for rhabdo; RR 1.78 for AKI (Ann Intern Med 2013)
- Illicit drugs: cocaine, amphetamines, heroin, MDMA, PCP
- Alcohol
- Infections: influenza, COVID-19 (0.2-2.2%), Coxsackievirus, Legionella
- Metabolic: hypokalemia, hypophosphatemia, hyponatremia, DKA, hypothyroidism
- Hereditary: McArdle disease, CPT II deficiency, malignant hyperthermia susceptibility, muscular dystrophies
- Sickle cell trait (HR 1.54 in military, NEJM 2016)
- Immobilization (prolonged surgery, especially bariatric: 19.4% pooled prevalence if BMI > 50)

## Pathogenesis
- Muscle injury → disrupted ion channels → excessive Na+/Ca2+ influx → sustained contraction + protease/phospholipase activation → cell lysis
- Released contents: K+, myoglobin, CK, uric acid, PO4, LDH, AST, ALT
- AKI mechanisms:
  1. Renal vasoconstriction (volume depletion + RAAS activation + myoglobin scavenges NO)
  2. Tubular obstruction (myoglobin precipitates with Tamm-Horsfall protein in acidic urine)
  3. Direct proximal tubule toxicity (Fenton reaction: heme + H2O2 → ROS; Fe3+/Fe4+ redox cycling → lipid peroxidation)

## Evaluation
- CK: diagnostic standard; check q4h if > 60,000 U/L or renal toxicity suspected
- Serum: Cr, electrolytes (K+, Ca2+, PO4), uric acid, LFTs, CBC, toxicology screen, troponin
- Urine: dipstick (positive for blood without RBCs = myoglobinuria), sediment (brown pigmented casts, uric acid crystals)
- ECG: hyperkalemia monitoring
- Compartment pressures if clinical suspicion

## Electrolyte Abnormalities
- Hyperkalemia: life-threatening; monitor q4h if CK > 60,000
- Hypocalcemia: early phase (Ca deposited in damaged muscle); do NOT correct unless symptomatic (tetany, seizures) or hyperkalemia with ECG changes → risk of rebound hypercalcemia in recovery phase
- Hyperphosphatemia, hyperuricemia
- Metabolic acidosis (lactic acid from muscle)

## Management
- Treat underlying cause
- Aggressive IV fluids (0.9% NS): start ASAP; rate ~400 mL/h; target UO 1-3 mL/kg/h (up to 300 mL/h)
- Titrate fluids based on volume status and UO; may decrease after CK peaks and declines
- NaHCO3 for urine alkalinization: NOT routinely recommended (may worsen hypocalcemia, volume overload); may still be used in patients with systemic acidosis
- Hyperkalemia management: IV calcium, insulin + glucose, nebulized albuterol, K+ binders, dialysis if refractory
- RRT: if unable to correct volume overload, acidosis, or hyperkalemia
- Compartment syndrome: surgical consultation for fasciotomy
- Cardiac monitoring: continuous ECG if K+ >= 6.5 or ECG changes [Strong]
