# Acid-Base Disorders — Clinical Approach

## Exam Logic Summary

Acid-base is high-yield. The exam tests: (1) systematic ABG interpretation, (2) compensation formula application, (3) AG calculation + correction, (4) delta-delta ratio interpretation, (5) matching clinical scenario to specific disorder. Common distractor: treating compensation as a primary disorder.

**Textbook ref:** Brenner Ch. 17 (Acid-Base Disorders), Harrison Ch. 58 (Acidosis and Alkalosis), Daugirdas Ch. 9 (Acid-Base in Dialysis)

---

## Core Concepts

### Normal Values

| Parameter | Arterial | Venous |
|---|---|---|
| pH | 7.4 (7.35-7.45) | 7.38 (7.33-7.43) |
| PaCO2 | 35-45 mmHg | 45-51 mmHg |
| HCO3⁻ | 22-26 mmol/L | 24-28 mmol/L |
| PaO2 | 80-100 mmHg | 35-40 mmHg |

### Four Primary Disorders

| Disorder | pH | Primary change | Key parameter |
|---|---|---|---|
| Metabolic acidosis | < 7.35 | ↓ HCO3⁻ (< 22) | HCO3⁻ |
| Metabolic alkalosis | > 7.45 | ↑ HCO3⁻ (> 26) | HCO3⁻ |
| Respiratory acidosis | < 7.35 | ↑ PaCO2 (> 42) | PaCO2 |
| Respiratory alkalosis | > 7.45 | ↓ PaCO2 (< 38) | PaCO2 |

**Exam pearl:** Compensation NEVER normalizes pH. Normal pH + abnormal HCO3⁻/PaCO2 = mixed disorder (≥ 2 primary processes).

---

## Systematic ABG Interpretation (6 Steps)

1. **pH** → acidemia (< 7.35) or alkalemia (> 7.45)?
2. **Primary cause** → which parameter (HCO3⁻ or PaCO2) explains the pH direction?
3. **Expected compensation** → apply formula (see below)
4. **If metabolic acidosis → AG** = Na⁺ - (Cl⁻ + HCO3⁻)
5. **If AG elevated → delta-delta** = ΔAG / ΔHCO3⁻
6. **If AG elevated + ingestion suspected → osmolal gap**

## Compensation Formulas (MUST MEMORIZE)

| Disorder | Formula | Mixed if... |
|---|---|---|
| **Met acidosis** | PaCO2 = 1.5 × HCO3⁻ + 8 ± 2 (Winter's) | PaCO2 too high → +resp acidosis; too low → +resp alkalosis |
| **Met alkalosis** | ΔPaCO2 = 0.6 × ΔHCO3⁻ | Same pattern |
| **Resp acidosis (acute)** | ΔHCO3⁻ = 1 per 10 mmHg ΔPaCO2 | HCO3⁻ too high → +met alkalosis; too low → +met acidosis |
| **Resp acidosis (chronic)** | ΔHCO3⁻ = 4-5 per 10 mmHg ΔPaCO2 | Same |
| **Resp alkalosis (acute)** | ΔHCO3⁻ = 2 per 10 mmHg ΔPaCO2 | Same |
| **Resp alkalosis (chronic)** | ΔHCO3⁻ = 4-5 per 10 mmHg ΔPaCO2 | Same |

**Exam pearl:** Chronic = > 6-12 hours. Respiratory compensation = minutes; renal compensation onset = 6-12h, max = 3-5 days.

**Exam distractor:** "chronic" compensation values are LARGER than acute (kidney has time to generate/excrete HCO3⁻). If question says "COPD patient" → use chronic formula. If question says "acute asthma attack" → use acute formula.

## Anion Gap

AG = Na⁺ - (Cl⁻ + HCO3⁻). Normal 8-12 mmol/L.

### Albumin Correction (critical for nephrotic/cirrhosis/ICU)

Corrected AG = AG + 2.5 × (4 - observed albumin [g/dL])

- AG > 12 → anion gap metabolic acidosis (AGMA)
- AG ≤ 12 → non-AG (hyperchloremic) metabolic acidosis (NAGMA)

**Exam pearl:** Low albumin HIDES AG acidosis. A patient with albumin 2.0 g/dL and calculated AG of 10 actually has corrected AG of 15 → AGMA.

---

## Delta-Delta Ratio

Δ-Δ = (AG - 12) / (24 - HCO3⁻)

| Ratio | Interpretation | Exam scenario |
|---|---|---|
| 0.8-1.2 | Pure AGMA only | Straightforward DKA or lactic acidosis |
| < 0.7 | AGMA + concurrent NAGMA | DKA resolving on NS (hyperchloremic phase) |
| > 1.2 | AGMA + concurrent met alkalosis | Vomiting patient with lactic acidosis |

**Condition-specific:**
- Lactic acidosis: ratio ~1.6 (H⁺ buffered in bone/cells → HCO3⁻ drops less)
- DKA: ratio ~1:1 initially; < 1 after treatment (renal ketoacid excretion → hyperchloremic transition)
- Toluene intoxication: usually < 1

---

## Osmolal Gap

OG = measured osmolality - calculated osmolality

Calculated = 2 × Na⁺ + glucose/18 + BUN/2.8 + ethanol/4.6 (all mg/dL)

Normal: -10 to +10 mOsm/L

| Finding | Suspect |
|---|---|
| ↑ OG + ↑ AG | Methanol, ethylene glycol, propylene glycol |
| ↑ OG + normal AG | Ethanol, isopropyl alcohol, mannitol |

**Exam pearl:** Osmolal gap > 10 + AG acidosis → think toxic alcohol until proven otherwise.

## Differential Diagnosis

### AGMA Causes (mnemonic: GOLDMARK)

- **G**lycols (ethylene, propylene)
- **O**xoproline (pyroglutamic acid, acetaminophen)
- **L**actic acidosis
- **D**-lactic acidosis
- **M**ethanol
- **A**spirin (salicylates)
- **R**enal failure (advanced CKD/AKI)
- **K**etoacidosis (DKA, starvation, alcoholic)

Also: rhabdomyolysis (massive), liver failure (type B lactic)

### NAGMA Causes (mnemonic: USED CARP)

- **U**reteral diversions
- **S**aline infusion (dilutional)
- **E**ndocrine (adrenal insufficiency)
- **D**iarrhea (GI HCO3⁻ loss)
- **C**arbonic anhydrase inhibitors
- **A**mmonium chloride / Arginine HCl
- **R**enal tubular acidosis (types I, II, IV)
- **P**ancreatic/biliary fistulas

**Exam pearl for RTA:**

| Type | Defect | Urine pH | Serum K⁺ | Classic associations |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Type I (distal) | Cannot secrete H⁺ in collecting duct | > 5.5 | Low | Sjögren, amphotericin, lithium, nephrocalcinosis |
| Type II (proximal) | Cannot reabsorb HCO3⁻ in PCT | < 5.5 (after HCO3⁻ exhaustion) | Low | Fanconi, myeloma, carbonic anhydrase inhibitors |
| Type IV | Aldosterone deficiency/resistance | < 5.5 | HIGH | Diabetes (hyporenin-hypoaldo), ACEi/ARB, spironolactone, trimethoprim |

**Exam distractor:** Type I = HIGH urine pH (can't acidify); Type II = LOW urine pH once threshold exceeded; Type IV = hyperkalemia distinguishes from I/II.

### Metabolic Alkalosis — Chloride Classification

**Cl⁻-responsive** (urine Cl⁻ < 25 mEq/L) → responds to saline:
- Vomiting/NG suction (most common exam scenario)
- Post-diuretic state
- Post-hypercapnic alkalosis

**Cl⁻-resistant** (urine Cl⁻ > 40 mEq/L) → saline won't fix:
- Primary aldosteronism, Cushing
- Bartter (loop-like) / Gitelman (thiazide-like)
- Liddle syndrome
- Severe hypokalemia

**Exam pearl:** Bartter = hypercalciuria (like furosemide). Gitelman = hypocalciuria + hypomagnesemia (like thiazide). Both = metabolic alkalosis + hypokalemia + normal BP.

### Respiratory Acidosis

Central: drug OD (narcotics, BZD), obesity hypoventilation, stroke
Neuromuscular: MG, ALS, Guillain-Barré
Pulmonary: COPD (most common chronic), severe asthma, pneumothorax

### Respiratory Alkalosis

Most common acid-base disorder in ICU (sepsis, early).
Causes: anxiety, sepsis, liver failure, PE, pregnancy, aspirin OD (early = resp alkalosis; late = met acidosis)

## Management Pearls

### Metabolic Acidosis

- **Primary**: treat underlying cause
- **NaHCO3**: only for pH < 7.0-7.1 (controversial); goal pH 7.2, NOT normalization
  - HCO3⁻ space = [0.4 + (2.6 / actual HCO3⁻)] × body weight (kg)
  - Risks: K⁺/Ca²⁺ shifts, CO2 generation, volume overload
- **Dialysis**: severe acidosis + renal failure → KDIGO 2024 endorses acid-base disturbance as indication for RRT initiation in CKD
- **Exam pearl**: NaHCO3 in lactic acidosis is NOT recommended (BICAR-ICU showed benefit only in AKI subgroup)

### Metabolic Alkalosis

- **Cl⁻-responsive**: NS + KCl replacement (most common answer)
- **Acetazolamide**: 250-500 mg/day if Cr < 4 and not volume depleted (inhibits carbonic anhydrase → bicarbonaturia)
- **Acid infusion**: IV HCl (central line only) for severe (pH > 7.55) + hepatic encephalopathy/arrhythmia
- **Dialysis**: severe alkalosis + renal failure
- **Exam pearl**: post-hypercapnic alkalosis = don't give saline alone; the retained HCO3⁻ needs time + adequate Cl⁻ for renal excretion

### Respiratory Acidosis

- **O2 therapy**: goal SpO2 90% / PaO2 60 mmHg
- **Caution in COPD**: excess O2 → ↓ hypoxic drive → worsening hypercapnia
- **Ventilation**: NPPV first (esp COPD); intubation if pH < 7.25 or PaCO2 > 80
- **Avoid rapid PaCO2 normalization** → post-hypercapnic metabolic alkalosis

### Respiratory Alkalosis

- Usually benign; treat underlying cause
- Do NOT give NaHCO3 or acetate

## Nephrology-Specific Considerations

### Acid-Base in CKD/ESKD

- CKD stage 4-5: progressive met acidosis from ↓ ammoniagenesis + ↓ titratable acid excretion → NaHCO3 supplementation or dialysis
- KDIGO 2024: suggests maintaining serum HCO3⁻ ≥ 22 mmol/L in CKD
- HD: standard dialysate HCO3⁻ 32-38 mmol/L corrects pre-dialysis acidosis; post-dialysis alkalosis is common
- PD: lactate-based dialysate → metabolic alkalosis risk; HCO3⁻-based or mixed solutions preferred

### Acid-Base in Dialysis Patients (exam scenario)

- Pre-HD: AG metabolic acidosis (uremic acid retention) — typical HCO3⁻ 18-22
- Immediate post-HD: metabolic alkalosis (HCO3⁻ gain from dialysate)
- Interdialytic: gradual return to acidosis
- **Exam pearl**: HD patient with pH 7.50, HCO3⁻ 30, PaCO2 38 immediately after dialysis = expected post-dialysis alkalosis, not a disease

## VBG vs ABG (Exam Scenarios)

- VBG acceptable in stable patients (pH diff ~0.03, HCO3⁻ diff ~1)
- Venous PCO2 < 45 mmHg → reliably excludes arterial PCO2 > 50 mmHg
- **Unreliable in**: hemodynamic instability, mechanical ventilation
- **Exam pearl**: question says "ABG results" → use values directly. Question says "VBG" → know the ~0.03 pH offset exists but still usable for screening

---

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

1. **DKA on NS**: initial AGMA → delta-delta ~1 → after NS resuscitation, AG normalizes but HCO3⁻ still low → NAGMA (hyperchloremic transition). Delta-delta drops < 0.7.

2. **Aspirin overdose**: EARLY = respiratory alkalosis (central stimulation); LATE = AG metabolic acidosis (mitochondrial uncoupling). Mixed picture = classic.

3. **COPD exacerbation**: chronic respiratory acidosis (HCO3⁻ 30-34) → acute worsening → pH drops but HCO3⁻ was already elevated → must use chronic compensation formula to detect acute-on-chronic.

4. **Vomiting + diarrhea**: met alkalosis (vomiting) + NAGMA (diarrhea) → may have near-normal pH with mixed picture.

5. **Ethylene glycol poisoning**: AG acidosis + elevated osmolal gap + calcium oxalate crystals in urine. Treatment: fomepizole + HD.

6. **Bartter vs Gitelman**: both = met alkalosis + hypokalemia + normal BP. Bartter = hypercalciuria, childhood onset, like furosemide. Gitelman = hypocalciuria, hypomagnesemia, adolescent/adult onset, like thiazide.

7. **RTA differentiation**: give NH4Cl load or check urine pH + K⁺. Type I = can't acidify (urine pH > 5.5). Type IV = hyperkalemia (not hypokalemia). Type II = Fanconi (glycosuria, phosphaturia, aminoaciduria).
