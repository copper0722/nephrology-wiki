# Drug and Toxin-Induced Kidney Disease — Board Review

## Exam Logic Summary

Tests: (1) NSAID mechanisms (hemodynamic + interstitial + glomerular), (2) lithium nephrotoxicity (NDI + chronic TIN), (3) anticoagulant-related nephropathy pathology, (4) gadolinium and NSF risk factors, (5) pigment nephropathy (rhabdomyolysis vs hemolysis) prevention and management. Common distractor: attributing all NSAID nephrotoxicity to hemodynamic effects alone.

## NSAID-Associated Kidney Disease

### Three Distinct Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Pathology | Clinical | Onset | Reversibility |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Hemodynamic** | ↓ prostaglandin → afferent arteriolar constriction → ↓ GFR | Prerenal AKI; oliguria; ↑ sCr | Hours-days | Reversible with NSAID cessation |
| **Acute interstitial nephritis** | T-cell-mediated allergic interstitial nephritis | AKI + proteinuria (often nephrotic due to concurrent MCD); eosinophilia; rash (rare) | Weeks-months | Usually reversible; steroids may help |
| **Chronic interstitial nephritis** | Papillary necrosis + chronic TIN | Chronic CKD; sterile pyuria; sloughed papillae | Months-years | Often irreversible |

### NSAID-Associated Minimal Change Disease

- NSAID-induced AIN can present with concurrent MCD (nephrotic syndrome)
- Mechanism: T-cell dysregulation → cytokine-mediated podocyte injury
- **Exam pearl:** Patient on NSAIDs with AKI + nephrotic-range proteinuria → think AIN with MCD. NOT just hemodynamic effect (which does not cause proteinuria).

### Papillary Necrosis (mnemonic: NSAIDS)

Causes of papillary necrosis:
- **N** = NSAIDs
- **S** = Sickle cell disease
- **A** = Analgesic nephropathy (phenacetin)
- **I** = Infection (pyelonephritis)
- **D** = Diabetes
- **S** = Systemic vasculitis

### Risk Factors for NSAID Nephrotoxicity

- Pre-existing CKD
- Volume depletion / effective volume depletion (CHF, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome)
- Concomitant ACEi/ARB (triple whammy: ACEi/ARB + diuretic + NSAID)
- Elderly
- Multiple NSAIDs or prolonged use

**Exam pearl:** "Triple whammy" = ACEi/ARB + diuretic + NSAID → synergistic hemodynamic AKI. ACEi blocks efferent constriction, NSAID blocks afferent dilation, diuretic reduces volume. Result: critical ↓ GFR.

### COX-2 Selective Inhibitors

- Same renal hemodynamic risks as nonselective NSAIDs (COX-2 is constitutively expressed in kidney)
- COX-2 in macula densa mediates renin release and TGF
- COX-2 in medullary interstitial cells produces PGE2 for sodium/water excretion
- **Exam pearl:** COX-2 inhibitors do NOT protect against NSAID nephrotoxicity. Renal COX-2 is constitutive, not inducible.

## Lithium-Associated Kidney Disease

### Overview

- Lithium is freely filtered and reabsorbed primarily in proximal tubule (60-70%) and collecting duct (via ENaC)
- Chronic lithium use (years) → multiple renal manifestations

### Manifestations

| Disorder | Mechanism | Prevalence | Reversibility |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Nephrogenic DI (NDI)** | Li+ enters collecting duct cells via ENaC → ↓ AQP2 expression → impaired water reabsorption | 20-40% of chronic users | Partially reversible (may persist years after cessation) |
| **Chronic tubulointerstitial nephritis** | Direct tubular toxicity → interstitial fibrosis | 15-20% develop CKD | Irreversible if advanced |
| **Distal RTA (incomplete)** | Impaired H+ secretion in collecting duct | Common | Partially reversible |
| **Hypercalcemia** | PTH-independent mechanism (lithium raises calcium set-point for PTH suppression) → hyperparathyroidism | 10-15% | May persist after lithium cessation |
| **Nephrotic syndrome** | MCD or FSGS (rare) | Rare | Variable |

### NDI — Key Points

- Most common renal effect of lithium
- Polyuria (> 3 L/day), polydipsia, nocturia
- Urine osmolality < 300 mOsm/kg despite elevated serum osmolality
- Does NOT respond to desmopressin (distinguishes from central DI)
- Treatment: amiloride (blocks ENaC → ↓ Li+ entry into collecting duct cells; first-line), thiazide (paradoxical antidiuresis), adequate hydration
- **Exam pearl:** Amiloride is the drug of choice for lithium-induced NDI because it blocks the entry point of lithium into collecting duct cells (ENaC). This both treats the NDI and may protect against further lithium toxicity.

### CKD Risk

- Risk of ESKD: ~1-2% of chronic lithium users (cohort studies)
- Duration-dependent: risk increases after > 10 years of use
- Characteristic biopsy: chronic tubulointerstitial nephritis with tubular atrophy, interstitial fibrosis, and **microcysts** (pathognomonic)
- Decision to continue lithium: balance psychiatric benefit vs CKD risk. Nephrologist + psychiatrist shared decision.

### Lithium Toxicity

- Narrow therapeutic index: therapeutic 0.6-1.2 mEq/L; toxic > 1.5 mEq/L
- Precipitants: dehydration, NSAIDs, ACEi/ARB, thiazides (all ↓ lithium clearance)
- Treatment: IV NS (enhance renal excretion), HD for severe toxicity (Li+ level > 4 mEq/L or severe symptoms)

## Anticoagulant-Related Nephropathy (ARN)

### Key Facts

- AKI caused by excessive anticoagulation → glomerular hemorrhage → tubular obstruction by RBC casts
- Originally described with warfarin ("warfarin-related nephropathy") but occurs with all anticoagulants (DOACs, heparin)
- Mechanism: supratherapeutic anticoagulation → hemorrhage into Bowman space → RBC casts in tubules → tubular obstruction + direct heme toxicity

### Risk Factors

- CKD (strongest risk factor — ↑ risk 5-10x)
- Supratherapeutic INR > 3.0 (warfarin)
- Age > 65
- Diabetes
- Heart failure
- Hypertension

### Pathology

- Glomerular hemorrhage (RBCs in Bowman space)
- **Occluding RBC casts** in tubules (pathognomonic)
- Acute tubular injury
- Interstitial hemorrhage may be present
- Often superimposed on underlying CKD pathology

### Clinical Presentation

- Unexplained AKI in patient on anticoagulant
- INR often (but not always) supratherapeutic at time of AKI
- Hematuria (gross or microscopic)
- No significant proteinuria (distinguishes from glomerulonephritis)
- Often misdiagnosed as other causes of AKI → biopsy needed for definitive diagnosis

### Management

- Correct over-anticoagulation (reverse warfarin with vitamin K; hold DOAC)
- Supportive AKI care
- Reassess indication for anticoagulation
- Consider alternative anticoagulant with lower bleeding risk
- Prognosis: many do not recover baseline renal function; accelerated CKD progression reported

**Exam pearl:** Unexplained AKI + supratherapeutic INR + hematuria + underlying CKD → anticoagulant nephropathy. Biopsy shows RBC casts occluding tubules. This is NOT just ATN from hypotension.

## Gadolinium-Associated Complications

### Nephrogenic Systemic Fibrosis (NSF)

- Systemic fibrotic condition affecting skin, joints, internal organs
- **Exclusively in patients with severely reduced kidney function** (GFR < 30, especially dialysis patients)
- Caused by release of free gadolinium (Gd3+) from chelate in setting of prolonged exposure (slow clearance)

### Risk Classification of Gadolinium Agents

| Risk category | Agents | Structure |
|---|---|---|
| **High risk (Group I)** | Gadodiamide (Omniscan), gadopentetate dimeglumine (Magnevist), gadoversetamide (OptiMARK) | Linear, non-ionic (least stable) |
| **Moderate risk (Group II)** | Gadobenate dimeglumine (MultiHance), gadoxetate (Eovist), gadofosveset (Ablavar) | Linear, ionic |
| **Low risk (Group III)** | Gadobutrol (Gadavist), gadoterate meglumine (Dotarem), gadoteridol (ProHance) | Macrocyclic (most stable) |

### Prevention

- **GFR < 30:** avoid Group I agents entirely; use Group III if contrast necessary
- **Dialysis patients:** if GBCA needed, use Group III + HD within 2-3 hours after administration (x3 sessions)
- GFR 30-60: Group III generally safe; screening not required by most guidelines
- GFR > 60: no NSF risk; no screening needed

### Acute Kidney Injury from Gadolinium

- Gadolinium-associated AKI is much rarer than contrast-induced AKI from iodinated contrast
- Most studies show no significant increase in AKI risk with GBCA at standard doses
- Risk may exist with very high doses or severe baseline CKD

**Exam pearl:** Gadolinium-based contrast ≠ iodinated contrast for AKI risk. The primary concern with gadolinium in CKD is NSF, not AKI. Use macrocyclic agents (Group III) for patients with GFR < 30.

## Pigment-Induced Kidney Injury

### Rhabdomyolysis

**Causes (mnemonic: CRIMES):**
- **C**rush injury, Compartment syndrome, Cocaine
- **R**ecreational drugs (statins at high dose, alcohol)
- **I**nfection, Ischemia, Immobilization
- **M**etabolic (hypokalemia, hypophosphatemia, hypothyroidism, DKA)
- **E**xercise (extreme), Eclampsia, Electrical injury
- **S**eizures, Snake bites, Serotonin syndrome, Status epilepticus

**Pathophysiology:**
- Myocyte injury → release of myoglobin, CK, K+, phosphate, uric acid, LDH
- Myoglobin precipitates in tubules (especially in acidic urine) → tubular obstruction
- Free iron from myoglobin → oxidative tubular injury
- Vasoconstriction (myoglobin scavenges NO)
- AKI occurs in 15-33% of rhabdomyolysis cases

**Diagnosis:**
- CK > 5x ULN (usually > 5,000 U/L; AKI risk increases significantly when CK > 15,000-20,000)
- Myoglobinuria (urine dipstick positive for "blood" but no RBCs on microscopy)
- Dark brown urine ("tea-colored")
- Hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia (early), hypercalcemia (recovery phase), ↑ uric acid, metabolic acidosis

**Management:**
- **Aggressive IV NS** (goal urine output 200-300 mL/h in adults) — cornerstone of treatment
- Start within 6 hours of injury for best outcomes
- Target: prevent tubular myoglobin precipitation
- **NaHCO3 for urine alkalinization:** controversial. Theoretically prevents myoglobin precipitation (less toxic at pH > 6.5). Risk: worsening hypocalcemia. NOT routinely recommended by most guidelines.
- **Mannitol:** not routinely recommended; may help if oliguria persists despite volume resuscitation
- Monitor and treat hyperkalemia (most dangerous acute complication)
- **RRT** if severe AKI with refractory hyperkalemia, volume overload, or severe acidosis
- Avoid calcium replacement for hypocalcemia unless symptomatic (risk of metastatic calcification during recovery when Ca rebounds)
- Treat compartment syndrome emergently (fasciotomy)

**Exam pearl:** Rhabdomyolysis AKI management = early aggressive NS. The debate over NaHCO3 is a common exam question — know that evidence is weak and it may worsen hypocalcemia. CK > 15,000 = high AKI risk.

### Hemoglobin-Induced Kidney Injury

- Intravascular hemolysis releases free hemoglobin → similar tubular toxicity as myoglobin
- Causes: transfusion reactions, malaria, G6PD deficiency, mechanical heart valves, TMA
- Lower threshold for AKI than myoglobin (hemoglobin more directly nephrotoxic)
- Management similar: IV fluids, urine alkalinization, treat underlying cause
- Urine dipstick positive for "blood" with no RBCs (same as myoglobinuria)

**Distinguishing myoglobinuria from hemoglobinuria:**
- Serum color: hemolysis → pink/red serum (free hemoglobin); rhabdomyolysis → clear serum (myoglobin cleared quickly)
- CK: markedly elevated in rhabdomyolysis; normal/mildly elevated in hemolysis
- LDH: elevated in both
- Haptoglobin: ↓ in hemolysis; normal in rhabdomyolysis

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

1. **Patient on ACEi + furosemide + ibuprofen develops AKI:** "Triple whammy." NSAID blocks afferent dilation (PGE2), ACEi blocks efferent constriction (AngII), diuretic depletes volume → critical ↓ GFR. Stop NSAID. Volume resuscitate.

2. **Patient on NSAIDs for 3 months develops nephrotic syndrome + AKI:** NSAID-induced AIN with concurrent MCD. Biopsy shows interstitial inflammation + podocyte effacement. Stop NSAID. Consider steroids for AIN. MCD responds to NSAID cessation.

3. **Lithium patient with polyuria 5 L/day, urine osm 180 mOsm/kg, does NOT respond to DDAVP:** Lithium-induced NDI. Start amiloride. Consider thiazide if amiloride insufficient. Monitor lithium levels closely (amiloride may ↑ levels).

4. **Dialysis patient needs MRI with contrast:** Use macrocyclic GBCA (Group III: gadobutrol, gadoterate). Schedule HD within 2-3 hours post-contrast x3 sessions. Avoid Group I agents (gadodiamide, gadopentetate).

5. **Crush injury, CK 45,000, K+ 6.8, sCr 4.5, dark urine:** Rhabdomyolysis with AKI. Aggressive NS (200-300 mL/h urine output). Treat hyperkalemia emergently (calcium gluconate, insulin/glucose, kayexalate). RRT if refractory. Do NOT routinely replace calcium.

6. **Patient on warfarin, INR 8.5, develops unexplained AKI, hematuria, baseline CKD:** Anticoagulant-related nephropathy. Reverse with vitamin K. Biopsy would show RBC casts in tubules, glomerular hemorrhage. Poor prognosis for renal recovery.
