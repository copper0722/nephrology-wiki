# Fluid Volume Disorders — Dehydration, Hypovolemia, and Edema

## Exam Logic Summary

Fluid volume assessment is high-yield, tested across nephrology, cardiology, and critical care. The exam tests: (1) distinguishing dehydration vs hypovolemia, (2) classifying by tonicity (iso/hypo/hypertonic), (3) FENa interpretation, (4) edema differential (cardiac vs hepatic vs nephrotic vs local), (5) diuretic strategy and resistance, (6) IV fluid selection. Common distractors: treating all volume depletion with NS, ignoring overcorrection risk in hyponatremic hypovolemia, confusing "dehydration" with "hypovolemia."

**Textbook ref:** Brenner Ch. 15 (Disorders of Sodium and Water), Harrison Ch. 49 (Fluid and Electrolyte Disturbances), Daugirdas Ch. 10 (Dry Weight and Ultrafiltration)

---

## Core Concepts: Dehydration vs Hypovolemia

| Term | Definition | Fluid lost | Compartment affected | Serum Na trend |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Dehydration** | Loss of predominantly water (hypotonic fluid) | Free water > electrolytes | ICF + ECF | ↑ (hypernatremia) |
| **Hypovolemia** | Loss of isotonic or sodium-rich fluid | Na + water proportionally | ECF (plasma + interstitial) | Normal or ↓ |

**Exam pearl:** These terms are often used interchangeably but are pathophysiologically distinct. Dehydration = water deficit → hypernatremia risk. Hypovolemia = volume deficit → hemodynamic compromise. Both can coexist.

## Body Fluid Distribution

| Compartment | Fraction of TBW | Volume (70 kg male) |
|---|---|---|
| Total body water (TBW) | 100% | ~42 L (60% body weight men, 50% women) |
| Intracellular | 2/3 of TBW | ~28 L |
| Extracellular | 1/3 of TBW | ~14 L |
| - Plasma | 25% of ECF | ~3.5 L |
| - Interstitial | 75% of ECF | ~10.5 L |

**Total body water estimation:**

| Group | TBW fraction |
|---|---|
| Children and young men | 0.6 x weight (kg) |
| Young women and older men | 0.5 x weight (kg) |
| Older women | 0.45 x weight (kg) |

**Exam pearl:** Clinically apparent edema requires interstitial volume increase of 2.5-3 L above normal. A patient can gain 3 kg of fluid before pitting edema becomes evident.

## Types of Hypovolemia by Tonicity

| Type | Serum Na | Serum Osm | Fluid lost | Classic causes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Isotonic** | 135-145 | 280-295 | Na + water proportionally | Hemorrhage, GI losses (diarrhea, vomiting), diuretics, third-spacing, surgical/trauma |
| **Hypertonic (hypernatremic)** | > 145 | > 295 | Water > Na (hypotonic loss) | Diabetes insipidus (central/nephrogenic), fever, burns, osmotic diuresis, inadequate water access, hyperosmolar tube feeds |
| **Hypotonic (hyponatremic)** | < 135 | < 270 | Na > water (then water replaced) | Diuretics + free water intake, adrenal insufficiency, cerebral salt wasting, salt-wasting nephropathy |

**Exam pearl:** Hypovolemia type determines treatment fluid. Isotonic → NS or LR. Hypertonic → NS first (correct ECF), then free water (D5W). Hypotonic → NS (replaces volume AND provides Na).

## Clinical Assessment of Volume Status

### History

**Screening mnemonic "DEHYDRATIONS":**
- **D**iuretics
- **E**nd of life
- **H**igh fever
- **Y**ellow urine (dark)
- **D**izziness (orthostasis)
- **R**educed oral intake
- **A**xilla dry
- **T**achycardia
- **I**ncontinence (fear of → reduced intake)
- **O**ral problems (sippers)
- **N**eurologic impairment (confusion)
- **S**unken eyes

### Physical Examination

| Sign | Sensitivity | Specificity | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Severe postural dizziness OR pulse increment >= 30/min | 97% (large blood loss) | 98% | Best overall for significant blood loss |
| Supine hypotension (SBP < 95) | 33% | 97% | High specificity, low sensitivity |
| Supine tachycardia (HR > 100) | 12% | 96% | Low sensitivity |
| Reduced subclavicular skin turgor | -- | High (OR 5.22) | More reliable than forearm turgor in elderly |
| Dry oral mucosa | -- | High (OR 6.84) | Confounded by mouth breathing, anticholinergics |
| Dry axilla | -- | High (>80%) | Useful in ER setting |
| Flat neck veins (supine) | -- | -- | Suggests low CVP |
| Prolonged capillary refill (> 3 sec; up to 4.5 sec in elderly) | -- | -- | Quick bedside test |
| Orthostatic hypotension (SBP drop > 20 or DBP > 10, or pulse ↑ 20) | Variable | Variable | Wait 2 min standing; absent in early/mild dehydration |

**Exam pearl:** In OLDER adults, classic dehydration signs (skin turgor, mucous membranes) may be absent or unreliable. Subclavicular skin turgor and axillary moisture are more reliable than forearm turgor. Weight change >= 3% over short period is a more objective indicator.

## Laboratory Evaluation

### Blood Tests

| Test | Hypovolemia finding | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| BUN/Cr ratio | > 20:1 (prerenal pattern) | Non-specific; elevated BUN also with GI bleeding, steroids, high protein |
| Serum Na | Indicates tonicity type | See table above |
| Serum osmolality | Isotonic: 280-295; Hypertonic: >295; Hypotonic: <270 | Measured osmolality preferred over calculated |
| Hematocrit | ↑ (hemoconcentration) | Unless concurrent hemorrhage |
| Serum HCO3 | May ↑ (contraction alkalosis) | From ECF volume contraction |

**Serum osmolality formula:**

Estimated Osm (mOsm/kg) = 2 x Na (mmol/L) + glucose (mg/dL)/18 + BUN (mg/dL)/2.8

Normal: 280-295 mOsm/kg

### Urine Studies (EXAM CRITICAL)

| Test | Extrarenal loss (prerenal) | Renal loss |
|---|---|---|
| **Urine Na** | < 20 mEq/L | > 20-30 mEq/L |
| **FENa** | < 1% | > 1% (> 2% most specific) |
| **Urine specific gravity** | > 1.016 | Variable |
| **Urine osmolality** | > 500 mOsm | Variable (300-500 in hyponatremic hypovolemia) |

**FENa = (urine Na x plasma Cr) / (plasma Na x urine Cr) x 100**

| FENa | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| < 1% | Prerenal azotemia (intact tubular reabsorption) — volume depletion, CHF, cirrhosis |
| > 2% | Intrinsic renal disease (ATN, intrinsic AKI) |
| 1-2% | Overlap zone — interpret with clinical context |

**FENa pitfalls (EXAM PEARLS):**
- FENa < 1% despite ATN: contrast nephropathy, rhabdomyolysis, early sepsis, pigment nephropathy
- FENa > 1% despite prerenal: diuretic use (must use FEUrea instead), CKD baseline, salt-wasting nephropathy
- **FEUrea < 35% = prerenal** even if on diuretics (urea reabsorption is diuretic-independent)
- Metabolic alkalosis (vomiting): use urine Cl instead of urine Na (bicarbonaturia drags Na into urine despite volume depletion → falsely high urine Na)

## Fluid Replacement

### Maintenance Fluids

- 24-hour maintenance = 1,500 mL + 20 mL/kg for each kg > 20 kg
- Or: 4-2-1 rule (4 mL/kg/hr first 10 kg, 2 mL/kg/hr next 10 kg, 1 mL/kg/hr remaining)

### IV Fluid Selection

| Fluid | Na (mEq/L) | Osmolality | Indication |
|---|---|---|---|
| Normal saline (0.9%) | 154 | 308 (isotonic) | Initial resuscitation, isotonic hypovolemia |
| Lactated Ringer | 130 | 273 (near-isotonic) | Resuscitation (less hyperchloremic acidosis than NS) |
| Half-normal saline (0.45%) | 77 | 154 (hypotonic) | Free water replacement in hypernatremic dehydration (after ECF corrected) |
| D5W | 0 | 252 (becomes free water) | Pure free water replacement |
| 3% saline | 513 | 1026 (hypertonic) | Severe symptomatic hyponatremia |

### Management by Hypovolemia Type

**Isotonic hypovolemia:**
- NS or LR for intravascular volume expansion
- 1 L crystalloid ≈ 250 mL stays intravascular

**Hypernatremic hypovolemia:**
1. First: isotonic saline to correct ECF volume depletion (restore hemodynamics)
2. Then: calculate free water deficit and replace with D5W or half-NS

**Water deficit (L) = TBW x ([serum Na / 140] - 1)**

3. Chronic hypernatremia (> 48 hr): correct slowly over 48-72 hours to avoid cerebral edema
4. Goal: lower Na by 8-10 mmol/day maximum
5. Add ongoing losses (insensible 30-50 mL/hr + urinary free water clearance)

**Hyponatremic hypovolemia:**
- Acute (< 48 hr): NS to correct volume; Na correction not restricted
- Chronic (> 48 hr): NS to correct volume, but monitor Na closely
  - Without ODS risk factors: correct Na <= 8 mEq/L/day
  - With ODS risk factors (Na <= 105, hypoK, alcoholism, malnutrition, liver disease): correct Na <= 6 mEq/L/day
  - **Watch for overcorrection:** once euvolemic, ADH suppresses → rapid aquaresis → Na rises too fast → give D5W to slow
  - Severe symptoms: 3% saline in first 6 hours, then reassess

**Exam pearl:** The most dangerous moment in hyponatremic hypovolemia is AFTER volume restoration — ADH shuts off → massive water diuresis → overcorrection → osmotic demyelination syndrome (ODS). Monitor q4-6h and be ready to give D5W.

## Edema — Approach

### Definition and Classification

- **Edema:** accumulation of fluid in interstitial space due to capillary filtration exceeding lymphatic drainage
- **Pitting edema:** indentation remains after pressure; indicates low-protein interstitial fluid (↓ oncotic pressure)
- **Non-pitting edema:** suggests lymphedema, myxedema, or lipedema
- **Generalized edema (anasarca):** systemic cause — cardiac, hepatic, renal
- **Localized edema:** structural/regional cause — venous, lymphatic, inflammatory

### Starling Forces (Pathophysiology)

Net filtration = Kf x [(Pc - Pi) - sigma x (pi_p - pi_i)]

Edema forms when:
1. ↑ capillary hydrostatic pressure (Pc) — CHF, venous obstruction, Na retention
2. ↓ plasma oncotic pressure (pi_p) — hypoalbuminemia (nephrotic, cirrhosis, malnutrition)
3. ↑ capillary permeability — inflammation, sepsis, burns, allergic reactions
4. Impaired lymphatic drainage — lymphedema, surgery, radiation, filariasis

### Edema Differential Diagnosis (EXAM TABLE)

| Cause | Mechanism | Key distinguishing features |
|---|---|---|
| **Heart failure** | ↑ venous pressure → ↑ Pc; Na retention via RAAS | Elevated JVP, S3, pulmonary rales, hepatomegaly, orthopnea, BNP elevated |
| **Cirrhosis** | Splanchnic vasodilation → RAAS activation + portal HTN + ↓ albumin | Ascites predominant, spider angiomata, jaundice, caput medusae, low JVP (underfill) |
| **Nephrotic syndrome** | ↓ albumin → ↓ oncotic pressure; also ↑ tubular Na reabsorption (ENaC activation by plasmin) | Periorbital edema (morning), proteinuria > 3.5 g/day, hypoalbuminemia, hyperlipidemia |
| **Nephritic / CKD** | ↓ GFR → Na/water retention → ↑ Pc | Hypertension, hematuria, ↑ Cr, active sediment |
| **Drugs** | Various: vasodilation (CCB, minoxidil), Na retention (NSAIDs, steroids, TZDs), capillary leak | Temporal relationship to drug initiation |
| **DVT** | Venous obstruction → ↑ local Pc | Acute, UNILATERAL, pain, warmth, swelling; Homan sign unreliable |
| **Lymphedema** | Impaired lymphatic return | Non-pitting, positive Stemmer sign (cannot tent skin on dorsum of toe), no response to elevation initially |
| **Myxedema** | Mucopolysaccharide deposition in dermis | Non-pitting, pretibial (Graves) or generalized (hypothyroidism); dry thick skin |
| **Idiopathic edema** | Capillary leak + RAAS activation (often diuretic-related) | Primarily premenopausal women; worsens with standing; diagnosis of exclusion |

#### Cardiac vs Hepatic vs Nephrotic Edema (COMPARISON)

| Feature | Heart failure | Cirrhosis | Nephrotic syndrome |
|---|---|---|---|
| JVP | Elevated | Normal/low | Normal |
| Albumin | Normal or mildly low | Low | Very low (< 2.5) |
| BNP | Elevated | Normal | Normal |
| Urine | Low Na, ± proteinuria | Low Na | Heavy proteinuria |
| Distribution | Dependent (legs, sacrum) | Ascites >> legs | Periorbital + dependent |
| BP | Often elevated (or low if late HF) | Low (splanchnic vasodilation) | Variable (may be elevated in nephritic overlap) |
| RAAS | Activated (low output) | Activated (splanchnic pooling) | Variable |

### Diagnostic Workup for Generalized Edema

1. Blood chemistry: Cr, LFTs, serum albumin
2. BNP/NT-proBNP (heart failure screening)
3. Urinalysis (proteinuria → nephrotic)
4. Chest X-ray (pulmonary edema, cardiomegaly, pleural effusion)
5. ECG (ischemic changes, arrhythmia)
6. TSH (hypothyroidism → myxedema)
7. If bilateral lower extremity without systemic disease: consider chronic venous insufficiency (duplex US), lymphedema (lymphoscintigraphy)

**BNP thresholds for echocardiography referral:**
- Male without ankle edema: BNP > 130-220 or NT-proBNP > 390-660
- Female without ankle edema: BNP > 210-360 or NT-proBNP > 620-1,060
- With ankle edema: BNP > 100-180 or NT-proBNP > 190-520

**CXR findings in heart failure (diagnostic performance):**
- Interstitial edema: 27% sensitivity, 98% specificity (LR+ 12.67)
- Cephalization: 41% sensitivity, 96% specificity (LR+ 9.41)
- Cardiomegaly: 79% sensitivity, 80% specificity (LR+ 3.98)

### Diuretic Approach to Edema (HIGH YIELD)

**General principles:**
- Dietary Na restriction < 2,000 mg/day (prerequisite for diuretic efficacy)
- Loop diuretics (furosemide, bumetanide, torsemide) = most effective
- Short half-life → multiple daily doses needed for sustained natriuresis
- Threshold dose concept: must reach minimal effective dose before dose escalation works; switching loop diuretics at sub-threshold dose is futile

**Loop diuretic comparison:**

| Drug | Oral bioavailability | Half-life | Relative potency |
|---|---|---|---|
| Furosemide | 50% (variable) | 1.5-2 hr | 40 mg |
| Bumetanide | 80-100% | 1-1.5 hr | 1 mg |
| Torsemide | 80-100% | 3-4 hr | 20 mg |

**Exam pearl:** Torsemide has longest half-life and most predictable absorption. Furosemide has variable oral bioavailability (50%, range 10-100%), making IV-to-oral conversion unreliable without monitoring response.

**Diuretic resistance — causes and management:**

| Cause | Management |
|---|---|
| Non-adherence to Na restriction | Counsel; check 24h urine Na (> 100 mEq = non-adherent) |
| Inadequate dose | Titrate to threshold dose |
| Poor absorption (GI edema in CHF/cirrhosis) | Switch to IV; or use torsemide (better absorption) |
| Reduced renal blood flow (low CO) | Treat underlying HF; consider inotropes |
| ↓ GFR (CKD) | Higher doses needed (furosemide 80-200 mg IV) |
| Nephron adaptation (chronic use) | Add thiazide (metolazone 2.5-5 mg 30 min before loop) — sequential nephron blockade |
| RAAS activation | Add spironolactone (especially cirrhosis) |
| NSAID use | Stop NSAIDs |
| Nephrotic syndrome | Hypoalbuminemia reduces delivery; some evidence for albumin-furosemide co-infusion |

**Sequential nephron blockade (EXAM CONCEPT):**
- Loop diuretic blocks Na reabsorption at TAL
- Chronic loop use → compensatory ↑ Na reabsorption at distal tubule (nephron remodeling)
- Adding thiazide (especially metolazone) blocks distal compensation
- Potent combination — monitor K, Mg, volume status closely

**Disease-specific diuretic strategy:**

| Condition | First-line | Key considerations |
|---|---|---|
| Heart failure | Loop diuretic (IV if acute) | Watch K, Mg; add spironolactone for mortality benefit (HFrEF) |
| Cirrhosis with ascites | Spironolactone (100 mg) + furosemide (40 mg) in 100:40 ratio | Spironolactone first (addresses secondary hyperaldosteronism); goal weight loss 0.5 kg/day without edema, 1 kg/day with |
| Nephrotic syndrome | Loop diuretic + Na restriction | Higher doses needed; albumin-furosemide debated; treat underlying GN |
| CKD | Loop diuretic (higher doses with lower GFR) | Thiazides ineffective at GFR < 30 (except metolazone); retained organic anions compete for tubular secretion |

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

1. **Hypernatremic dehydration with hypovolemia:** 78M nursing home, obtunded, Na 168, BUN 80, Cr 2.5, HR 110, BP 85/50. Answer: First isotonic saline to restore hemodynamics (treat shock first). Then calculate water deficit [0.5 x 70 x (168/140 - 1) = 7 L] and replace slowly over 48-72 hours with D5W. Target Na drop 8-10 mmol/day max. Add insensible losses (40 mL/hr) + urinary free water clearance.

2. **Hyponatremic hypovolemia with overcorrection risk:** 45F on HCTZ, Na 112, obtunded. NS started, 6 hours later Na 122 (+10). Answer: Overcorrection risk → give D5W immediately to slow correction. High-risk patient (Na < 120, thiazide). Goal <= 6-8 mEq/L/day. Once euvolemic, ADH suppression causes rapid aquaresis → the dangerous period. Consider desmopressin to prevent further rise.

3. **Edema differential — cardiac vs nephrotic:** 55M bilateral leg edema, periorbital puffiness, BP 140/90, albumin 1.8, Cr 1.2, urine protein 8 g/day, BNP 45. Answer: Nephrotic syndrome (heavy proteinuria, hypoalbuminemia, periorbital edema, normal BNP rules out CHF). Next: lipid panel, renal biopsy. Treatment: treat GN + loop diuretic + Na restriction.

4. **Diuretic resistance in cirrhosis:** 60M cirrhosis, ascites, on furosemide 80 mg BID with minimal response, K 2.8. Answer: Start spironolactone (secondary hyperaldosteronism not being addressed). Classic cirrhosis regimen: spironolactone:furosemide ratio 100:40. The hypoK confirms aldosterone excess. Also check dietary Na compliance (24h urine Na).

5. **FENa interpretation pitfall:** 72F on furosemide, presenting with AKI (Cr 1.0 → 3.5). FENa 3.2%. Is this ATN? Answer: Cannot interpret FENa on diuretics — diuretics increase FENa regardless of prerenal status. Use FEUrea instead. FEUrea < 35% = prerenal despite high FENa. If FEUrea > 50% = intrinsic renal (ATN).
