# Genetic and Structural Tubulopathies — Board Review

## Exam Logic Summary

Tests: (1) ADTKD as autosomal dominant tubulointerstitial disease with bland urine, (2) cystinosis as most common cause of Fanconi syndrome in children, (3) medullary sponge kidney associations and imaging, (4) LPHS as diagnosis of exclusion. Common distractor: confusing ADTKD with ADPKD.

## Autosomal Dominant Tubulointerstitial Kidney Disease (ADTKD)

### Key Facts

- Previously: medullary cystic kidney disease type 2 (MCKD2), familial juvenile hyperuricemic nephropathy, uromodulin kidney disease
- **Autosomal dominant** inheritance → progressive tubulointerstitial fibrosis → ESKD
- Renamed ADTKD by KDIGO 2015 consensus; classified by causative gene

### Classification by Gene

| Subtype | Gene | Protein | Distinguishing features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **ADTKD-UMOD** | UMOD | Uromodulin (Tamm-Horsfall) | Hyperuricemia + gout (young age); most common subtype |
| **ADTKD-MUC1** | MUC1 | Mucin 1 | No hyperuricemia; diagnosis requires specialized genetic testing |
| **ADTKD-REN** | REN | Renin | Childhood anemia, hyperkalemia, mild hypotension |
| **ADTKD-HNF1B** | HNF1B | Hepatocyte nuclear factor 1-beta | MODY diabetes, CAKUT, hypomagnesemia, hyperuricemia |
| **ADTKD-SEC61A1** | SEC61A1 | Translocon subunit | Anemia, neutropenia (very rare) |

### Clinical Features (All Subtypes)

- Slow progressive CKD → ESKD typically in 30s-70s (variable)
- **Bland urine sediment** (no hematuria, minimal proteinuria)
- **No hypertension** early (develops with CKD progression)
- Normal or small kidneys (NO large cysts like ADPKD)
- Corticomedullary cysts may be present but NOT required for diagnosis
- Family history of CKD with AD inheritance pattern

**Exam pearl:** AD family history + progressive CKD + bland urine + no cysts/small kidneys + young-onset gout → ADTKD-UMOD until proven otherwise. Contrast with ADPKD (large kidneys, numerous cysts, hematuria, HTN).

### ADTKD-UMOD Specific

- Uromodulin = most abundant urinary protein; produced exclusively by thick ascending limb
- Mutant uromodulin misfolds → retained in ER → cell stress → tubulointerstitial inflammation → fibrosis
- **Hyperuricemia** (often < 2 mg/dL urate clearance) → gout in teens/young adults
- Reduced uromodulin excretion in urine (can be measured)
- ESKD: median age 54 years (range 17-74)

### Diagnosis

- **Genetic testing:** definitive diagnosis (UMOD, MUC1, REN, HNF1B, SEC61A1)
- MUC1 mutation requires specialized assay (standard sequencing may miss it)
- Kidney biopsy: nonspecific tubulointerstitial fibrosis, tubular atrophy; NOT diagnostic but can exclude other causes

### Management

- No disease-specific treatment currently
- Standard CKD management (BP control, avoid nephrotoxins)
- Allopurinol/febuxostat for hyperuricemia/gout — does NOT slow CKD progression
- Kidney transplant: disease does NOT recur in graft (not a systemic disease affecting transplant)

## Cystinosis

### Key Facts

- **Autosomal recessive** lysosomal storage disease caused by CTNS gene mutations → defective cystinosin → cystine accumulation in lysosomes of multiple organs
- Most common cause of **Fanconi syndrome** in children (exam classic)
- Most common cause of inherited proximal RTA in children

### Types

| Type | Onset | Severity | ESKD |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Infantile (nephropathic)** | 6-12 months | Most common (95%), most severe | By age 10 without treatment |
| Late-onset (juvenile) | Adolescence | Intermediate | Variable |
| Non-nephropathic (ocular) | Adulthood | Mildest; corneal crystals only | No renal involvement |

### Clinical Features — Infantile (Nephropathic) Cystinosis

**Renal manifestations:**
- **Fanconi syndrome** (proximal tubular dysfunction): glycosuria, phosphaturia, aminoaciduria, bicarbonaturia (proximal RTA), low-molecular-weight proteinuria, uricosuria, sodium/potassium wasting
- Polyuria, polydipsia (concentrating defect)
- Hypophosphatemic rickets (phosphate wasting)
- Growth failure (multiple mechanisms: renal losses, metabolic acidosis, hypothyroidism)
- Progressive CKD → ESKD by age 10 (without cysteamine treatment)

**Extrarenal manifestations:**
- **Corneal cystine crystals** (pathognomonic; slit-lamp exam)
- Hypothyroidism (cystine deposits in thyroid)
- Photophobia and visual impairment
- Hepatomegaly (late)
- Myopathy, swallowing difficulty
- CNS involvement (later)
- Pancreatic exocrine and endocrine dysfunction (diabetes)

**Exam pearl:** Infant with failure to thrive + Fanconi syndrome (NAGMA + glycosuria + phosphaturia + aminoaciduria) + corneal crystals → cystinosis. Measure WBC cystine levels to confirm.

### Diagnosis

- **WBC cystine level:** elevated (> 2 nmol half-cystine/mg protein) — confirmatory test
- Corneal slit-lamp exam: refractile cystine crystals (can be absent in infants < 1 year)
- Genetic testing: CTNS mutations (most common: 57-kb deletion in European populations)

### Treatment

- **Cysteamine** (oral): depletes lysosomal cystine by forming cysteine-cysteamine mixed disulfide → exits lysosome via different transporter
  - Must be started early (before significant renal damage)
  - Delays progression to ESKD by years (from age 10 to age 20+)
  - Dose: 1.3 g/m2/day divided Q6H (immediate-release) or Q12H (delayed-release)
  - Side effects: GI upset, body odor, breath odor
- **Cysteamine eye drops** (0.44%): for corneal crystals (topical, Q waking hours)
- Fanconi syndrome management: phosphate supplements, vitamin D (calcitriol), alkali for acidosis, potassium replacement, carnitine
- Growth hormone for growth failure
- Thyroid hormone replacement as needed
- Kidney transplant: cystinosis does NOT recur in graft (CTNS mutation in host cells, not graft); however, extrarenal disease continues

## Medullary Sponge Kidney

### Key Facts

- Congenital disorder with cystic dilation of medullary and papillary collecting ducts
- Usually asymptomatic; discovered incidentally
- Bilateral in 60-80% of cases
- **NOT progressive** to CKD in most cases (benign prognosis)
- Sporadic (most cases); rare familial forms reported

### Clinical Features

- Often asymptomatic (incidental finding)
- **Nephrocalcinosis** (calcium deposits in medullary pyramids) — most characteristic finding
- Recurrent **calcium kidney stones** (most common complication)
- Hematuria (gross or microscopic)
- Recurrent UTIs
- Distal RTA (incomplete form) — impaired urinary acidification → contributes to stone formation
- Hypercalciuria (50% of patients)

### Diagnosis

- **IVP (intravenous pyelogram):** classic "paint brush" or "bouquet of flowers" pattern — contrast fills dilated collecting ducts radiating from papillae. Gold standard but rarely done now.
- **CT urogram:** can show medullary nephrocalcinosis and cystic changes but less sensitive than IVP for duct ectasia
- US: medullary hyperechogenicity (nephrocalcinosis); may miss subtle duct ectasia

**Exam pearl:** Medullary sponge kidney on imaging + recurrent calcium stones + distal RTA + normal renal function → classic presentation. IVP "bouquet of flowers" = pathognomonic.

### Associations

- Hemihypertrophy (Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome)
- Ehlers-Danlos syndrome
- Caroli disease (hepatic duct ectasia)
- Primary hyperparathyroidism

### Management

- Treat complications (stones, UTI, acidosis)
- High fluid intake (stone prevention)
- Thiazide diuretics for hypercalciuria
- Potassium citrate for RTA and hypocitraturia
- No specific treatment for the duct ectasia itself

## Loin Pain Hematuria Syndrome (LPHS)

### Key Facts

- Recurrent or persistent unilateral/bilateral loin (flank) pain + hematuria (gross or microscopic)
- Diagnosis of **exclusion** — must rule out all other causes
- Predominantly affects young women
- Pathophysiology unclear; theories: intrarenal vasospasm, C3 deposits in arteriolar walls, coagulopathy

### Diagnostic Criteria

All of the following:
1. Severe recurrent flank pain (often requiring narcotics)
2. Hematuria (micro or gross) during pain episodes
3. No identifiable cause after thorough workup (stones, tumor, infection, GN, vascular malformation ruled out)

### Workup Required Before Diagnosis

- Urine: microscopy, culture, cytology
- Imaging: CT urogram (rule out stones, masses, vascular malformations)
- Cystoscopy (if needed)
- Renal biopsy: usually normal or shows thin basement membrane or mild mesangial IgA; C3 deposits in arteriolar walls described

### Management (Difficult)

- Pain management (multidisciplinary)
- Avoid chronic narcotics if possible
- ACEi (for proteinuria if present)
- Autotransplantation: renal denervation by autotransplant to iliac fossa — variable results (pain recurrence in 30-50%)
- Capsaicin instillation into renal pelvis (investigational)
- **Exam pearl:** LPHS is a diagnosis of exclusion. The exam will test whether you can rule out all other causes before accepting this diagnosis.

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

1. **30-year-old with AD family history of CKD, gout since age 18, sCr 2.5, bland urine, normal-sized kidneys:** ADTKD-UMOD. Genetic testing for UMOD. No specific treatment. Allopurinol for gout. Transplant when ESKD (no recurrence).

2. **9-month-old with FTT, polyuria, NAGMA, glycosuria, phosphaturia, aminoaciduria:** Fanconi syndrome → most common cause in this age = cystinosis. Check WBC cystine level. Slit-lamp for corneal crystals. Start cysteamine immediately.

3. **40-year-old with recurrent calcium stones, IVP showing "bouquet of flowers" pattern in medullary pyramids:** Medullary sponge kidney. Check for distal RTA (urine pH, acid load test). Thiazide + K citrate for stone prevention.

4. **Differentiating ADTKD from ADPKD on exam:** ADTKD = normal/small kidneys, bland urine, early gout, AD inheritance. ADPKD = enlarged kidneys, multiple large cysts, hematuria, HTN, liver cysts, cerebral aneurysms. Both are AD but completely different diseases.
