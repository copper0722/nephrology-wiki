# Primary Glomerular Diseases

## Exam Logic Summary

Glomerular diseases are the core of nephrology board exams. The exam tests: (1) pattern recognition of nephrotic vs nephritic syndrome, (2) matching IF/EM findings to specific diseases, (3) complement level patterns, (4) serology interpretation (ANCA, anti-GBM, anti-PLA2R), (5) treatment algorithms by disease and risk category. The critical skill is integrating clinical presentation + serology + pathology into a single diagnosis.

**Key framework:** Nephrotic = proteinuria > 3.5 g/day, hypoalbuminemia, edema, hyperlipidemia, bland sediment. Nephritic = hematuria with RBC casts, HTN, reduced GFR, subnephrotic proteinuria. Some diseases cross both (IgAN, MPGN).

**Textbook ref:** Brenner Ch. 31-36 (Glomerular Diseases), KDIGO 2021 Glomerular Diseases Guideline

## Approach to Glomerular Disease

### Nephrotic vs Nephritic Syndrome

| Feature | Nephrotic | Nephritic |
|---|---|---|
| Proteinuria | > 3.5 g/day | < 3.5 g/day (usually) |
| Hematuria | Absent or mild | Active (dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts) |
| Sediment | Bland, oval fat bodies, fatty casts | Active: RBC casts, WBC casts |
| Albumin | < 3.5 g/dL (often < 2.5) | Mildly low or normal |
| Edema | Prominent (anasarca) | Present but less |
| BP | Often normal initially | Elevated |
| GFR | Often preserved initially | Reduced |
| Lipids | Elevated | Usually normal |
| Complement | Usually normal (except MPGN, lupus) | Often low |

### Conditions by Presentation

| Presentation | Diseases |
|---|---|
| **Pure nephrotic** | MCD, MN, FSGS, diabetic nephropathy, amyloidosis |
| **Nephritic** | IgAN, post-infectious GN, lupus nephritis class III/IV, ANCA vasculitis |
| **Nephrotic + nephritic** | MPGN, lupus nephritis class V+III/IV, IgAN (severe) |
| **RPGN** | Anti-GBM, ANCA vasculitis, severe IgAN, lupus nephritis |
| **Asymptomatic hematuria** | IgAN, thin basement membrane, Alport |

### Master Diagnostic Table: IF/EM/Serology

| Disease | IF Pattern | EM Deposits | Complement | Key Serology |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **MCD** | Negative (may have trace IgM/C3) | Foot process effacement only, no deposits | Normal | None |
| **FSGS** | IgM + C3 in sclerotic segments | Foot process effacement (diffuse in primary) | Normal | None (suPAR controversial) |
| **MN** | Granular IgG (IgG4) + C3 along GBM | Subepithelial deposits (spikes) | Normal | Anti-PLA2R (80%), anti-THSD7A (1-3%) |
| **MPGN** | C3 +/- Ig along GBM and mesangium | Subendothelial deposits (type I); intramembranous (DDD) | **Low C3** +/- low C4 | C3 nephritic factor (DDD); hepatitis C (type I) |
| **IgAN** | **Mesangial IgA** (dominant) +/- IgG, IgM, C3 | Mesangial electron-dense deposits | Normal | Elevated serum IgA (50%, not diagnostic) |
| **Lupus (V)** | "Full house" (IgG, IgA, IgM, C3, C1q) | Subepithelial deposits | Low C3 + C4 | ANA, anti-dsDNA, anti-Sm |
| **Anti-GBM** | **Linear IgG** along GBM | No deposits | Normal | Anti-GBM antibodies |
| **ANCA** | **Pauci-immune** (neg or faint IF) | No deposits | Normal | PR3-ANCA (GPA), MPO-ANCA (MPA) |
| **Post-infectious** | "Starry sky" C3 + IgG, subepithelial humps | Subepithelial humps | Low C3 (normalizes 6-8 wk) | ASO, anti-DNase B |

**Exam pearl:** If complement is low, think MPGN, lupus, post-infectious GN, or cryoglobulinemia. If complement is normal with active sediment, think IgAN, ANCA, or anti-GBM.

**Exam pearl:** Linear IF = anti-GBM. Granular IF = immune complex. Pauci-immune (negative IF) = ANCA vasculitis. This is the single most tested pathology concept in glomerular disease.

## Minimal Change Disease (MCD)

### Key Facts

- Most common cause of nephrotic syndrome in children (70-90% of cases in children > 1 year)
- 10-25% of adult nephrotic syndrome
- Incidence: 0.6/100,000 adults; 2-15.6/100,000 children
- M:F = 2:1 in children; female predominance in adults
- Etiology unknown in most; associated with atopy, NSAIDs, Hodgkin lymphoma, lithium

### Diagnosis

- **Adults**: kidney biopsy REQUIRED to confirm
- **Children**: empiric steroid trial without biopsy (if age 1-12, no atypical features); biopsy only if steroid-resistant or atypical
- **LM**: Normal glomeruli (or minimal mesangial proliferation)
- **IF**: Usually negative; may have trace IgM, C3, or C1q staining
- **EM**: Diffuse foot process effacement WITHOUT electron-dense deposits
- Complement levels: NORMAL (low C3 should prompt workup for MPGN or lupus)

**Exam pearl:** MCD is a diagnosis of EXCLUSION on biopsy. If ANY additional pathological features present (sclerosis, IgA deposits, C1q dominant), the diagnosis is something else. Early FSGS can be missed on small biopsy samples.

**Exam distractor:** "Child with sudden-onset edema, proteinuria 8 g/day, normal complement, bland sediment" = MCD until proven otherwise. No need for biopsy in typical presentation age 1-12.

### Variants

- **IgM nephropathy**: dominant mesangial IgM on IF; steroid-resistant, worse prognosis
- **C1q nephropathy**: dominant mesangial C1q on IF; older children/young adults; variable response to steroids

### Treatment (KDIGO 2021)

**Adults - initial episode:**
- High-dose corticosteroids: prednisone 1 mg/kg/day (max 80 mg) or 2 mg/kg QOD (max 120 mg) for 4-16 weeks, taper over >= 24 weeks [KDIGO Level 1, Grade C]
- If contraindication to steroids: cyclophosphamide, CNI (cyclosporine/tacrolimus), MMF, or rituximab

**Adults - frequently relapsing/steroid-dependent:**
- Re-treat with steroids until remission, then add steroid-sparing agent
- Options: cyclophosphamide 2-2.5 mg/kg/day x 8-12 weeks (max cumulative 168 mg/kg), or levamisole x >= 12 months
- Rituximab emerging as primary choice

**Adults - steroid-resistant:**
- Repeat biopsy (to exclude FSGS)
- CNI as second-line: cyclosporine 5 mg/kg/day (target trough 60-150 ng/mL) or tacrolimus 0.1 mg/kg/day (target trough 5-10 ng/mL) for >= 12 months [KDIGO Level 1, Grade C]

**Children:**
- Prednisone/prednisolone 60 mg/m2/day (max 60 mg) for 4-6 weeks, then 40 mg/m2 QOD for 4-6 weeks [KDIGO Level 1, Grade B]
- Steroid-resistant: CNI as second-line + low-dose steroids
- Frequent relapsers: steroid-sparing agents (cyclophosphamide, levamisole, CNI, rituximab)

## Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS)

### Key Facts

- Histologic pattern (not a single disease) characterized by podocyte injury, depletion, and segmental sclerosis
- 7-10% of childhood, 20-30% of adult nephrotic syndrome
- Incidence: 0.8/100,000; prevalence 38.9% of biopsy-proven primary GN
- More common in males (1.5-2x); higher incidence in Black patients (APOL1)
- Three types: primary (idiopathic), secondary (adaptive/drug/viral), genetic

### Primary vs Secondary FSGS (Exam Critical)

| Feature | Primary FSGS | Secondary FSGS |
|---|---|---|
| Onset | Abrupt nephrotic syndrome | Gradual proteinuria, often subnephrotic |
| Nephrotic syndrome | 54-100% | Uncommon |
| Serum albumin | Low | Often normal |
| Foot process effacement | Diffuse (> 80%) | Focal/segmental |
| Mechanism | Circulating permeability factor | Hyperfiltration/maladaptation |
| Recurrence post-transplant | 30-40% | Rare |
| Treatment | Immunosuppression | Treat underlying cause + RAAS blockade |

**Exam pearl:** Secondary FSGS from obesity/reduced nephron mass does NOT respond to immunosuppression. Look for "subnephrotic proteinuria with normal albumin" as the clue for secondary FSGS.

### Genetic FSGS

- NPHS1 (nephrin): Finnish-type congenital NS, AR
- NPHS2 (podocin): childhood FSGS, AR
- APOL1 G1/G2: African descent, adult-onset, rapid progression, does NOT respond to immunosuppression
- COL4A3/A4/A5: Alport spectrum
- WT1: Denys-Drash/Frasier syndrome

**Exam pearl:** APOL1 high-risk genotype (2 risk alleles): associated with collapsing FSGS variant, HIV-associated nephropathy, and COVID-associated collapsing glomerulopathy in patients of African descent.

### Histologic Variants (Columbia Classification)

| Variant | Key Feature | Prognosis |
|---|---|---|
| NOS (not otherwise specified) | Most common | Intermediate |
| Perihilar | Sclerosis at vascular pole | Often secondary/adaptive |
| Cellular | Endocapillary hypercellularity | Better response to steroids |
| Tip | Sclerosis at tubular pole | Best prognosis, steroid-responsive |
| Collapsing | Global collapse of capillary tuft | Worst prognosis; HIV, APOL1, COVID |

### Treatment (KDIGO 2021)

**Primary FSGS:**
- First-line: high-dose corticosteroids: prednisone 1 mg/kg (max 80 mg) daily or 2 mg/kg (max 120 mg) QOD for >= 4 weeks, continue up to 16 weeks [Strong Recommendation]
- Alternative first-line (if steroid contraindication): CNI (cyclosporine 3-5 mg/kg/day or tacrolimus 0.05-0.1 mg/kg/day); target cyclosporine trough 100-175 ng/mL, tacrolimus 5-10 ng/mL
- Steroid-resistant: CNI for >= 6 months [Strong Recommendation]
- Relapse after steroids: cyclophosphamide (if no prior CYC), rituximab, CNI, or MMF [Strong Recommendation]

**Secondary FSGS:**
- Treat underlying cause (stop offending drug, treat infection, weight loss for obesity)
- NO immunosuppression
- RAAS blockade (ACEi/ARB) to reduce hyperfiltration
- SGLT2 inhibitors for proteinuria reduction

**Recurrent FSGS post-transplant:**
- Plasma exchange and immunoadsorption
- Rituximab, ofatumumab, abatacept, belatacept reported
- Preconditioning regimens for high-risk patients

## Membranous Nephropathy (MN)

### Key Facts

- Most common cause of adult nephrotic syndrome in non-diabetic patients (20-40%)
- Incidence: 1.2/100,000 per year
- M:F = 2:1; median onset early 50s
- Primary (80%): anti-PLA2R antibodies (70-80%), anti-THSD7A (1-3%)
- Secondary (20%): SLE (class V; anti-EXT1/EXT2), HBV, HCV, malignancy, drugs (NSAIDs, gold, penicillamine), sarcoidosis

### Diagnosis

- **Serology first**: anti-PLA2R antibodies (sensitivity 65%, specificity 97% by ELISA)
  - Positive anti-PLA2R = diagnostic of primary MN, may negate need for biopsy in typical presentation
  - Titers correlate with disease activity and predict relapse
- **If anti-PLA2R negative**: biopsy with PLA2R tissue staining (sensitivity 79%, specificity 90%)
- **LM**: Normal early; later GBM thickening with "spikes" on silver stain
- **IF**: Diffuse, granular IgG4 + C3 along capillary walls (subepithelial)
  - IgG1/IgG3 dominant or C1q+ suggests SECONDARY MN
- **EM**: Subepithelial electron-dense deposits

**Exam pearl:** "Granular IgG along GBM" = membranous. "Linear IgG along GBM" = anti-GBM. This is a frequently tested distinction.

**Exam pearl:** In secondary MN, look for: C1q positivity, mesangial/subendothelial deposits, IgG1/IgG3 (not IgG4), endothelial tubuloreticular inclusions (lupus).

### Histologic Findings Suggesting Secondary MN

- Mesangial and endothelial cell proliferation
- Crescents
- Tubular basement membrane staining
- Prominent IgG1/IgG3, IgM, or IgA deposition
- C1q or EXT1/EXT2 positivity
- Mesangial and subendothelial electron-dense deposits
- Endothelial tubuloreticular inclusions on EM

### Risk Stratification (KDIGO 2021)

| Risk | Criteria |
|---|---|
| **Low** | Normal eGFR, proteinuria < 3.5 g/day, albumin > 3 g/dL; OR proteinuria decreasing > 50% on conservative therapy |
| **Moderate** | Normal eGFR, proteinuria > 3.5 g/day, not decreasing > 50% after 6 months of conservative therapy |
| **High** | Proteinuria > 8 g/day for > 6 months AND/OR eGFR < 60; OR proteinuria > 3.5 g/day not decreasing after 6 months + albumin < 2.5 g/dL OR PLA2R > 50 RU/mL |
| **Very high** | Life-threatening nephrotic syndrome; unexplained rapid GFR decline |

### Treatment (KDIGO 2021)

**All patients**: supportive care
- BP < 130/80: ACEi/ARB (also reduces proteinuria)
- Na restriction, loop diuretics for edema
- Statin for hyperlipidemia
- Anticoagulation if high thrombosis risk (albumin < 2.5 g/dL)
- Consider SGLT2 inhibitor

**By risk:**
- **Low**: supportive care + observation (up to 1/3 spontaneously remit)
- **Moderate**: observation with supportive care OR rituximab OR CNI +/- corticosteroids
- **High**: rituximab, OR cyclophosphamide + corticosteroids (modified Ponticelli), OR CNI + rituximab
- **Very high**: cyclophosphamide + corticosteroids

**Monitor:** anti-PLA2R titers to evaluate treatment response (serologic remission precedes clinical remission by months)

**Relapsed/refractory:**
- Repeat same therapy if prior good response
- Switch class if inadequate initial response (e.g., rituximab to CYC+steroids or vice versa)
- Consider rituximab + CNI combination

## Membranoproliferative Glomerulonephritis (MPGN)

### Key Facts

- NOT a single disease but a histologic PATTERN of injury
- Two pathogenic mechanisms: immune complex-mediated (classical pathway) vs complement-mediated (C3 glomerulopathy = alternative pathway)
- Typically children and young adults
- Incidence: 0.14-0.93/100,000; declining due to decreased HCV prevalence
- 7-10% of biopsy-proven GN

### Contemporary Classification (Critical for Exam)

| Category | IF Finding | Mechanism | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Immune complex-mediated** | Ig + complement (IgG, IgM, C3, kappa/lambda) | Classical complement activation | HCV, HBV, autoimmune (SLE, Sjogren), monoclonal gammopathy, endocarditis |
| **Complement-mediated (C3 glomerulopathy)** | C3 dominant (no/minimal Ig) | Alternative complement pathway dysregulation | C3 GN, dense deposit disease (DDD); genetic (CFH, CFI, CFB mutations) or acquired (C3 nephritic factor) |
| **Ig and complement-negative** | Negative | TMA healing, APS, others | Drug-associated TMA, sickle cell |

**Exam pearl:** The OLD classification (type I/II/III based on EM deposit location) is OBSOLETE. Current classification is based on IF: immune complex vs complement-dominant vs negative. This is a frequently tested concept -- exams may use old terminology as a distractor.

### Diagnosis

- Presentation: nephrotic syndrome (40-70%), nephritic syndrome (20-30%), or mixed; hypocomplementemia (low C3 +/- C4)
- **LM**: Diffuse mesangial cell proliferation + capillary wall thickening; "tram-track" or lobular appearance on PAS/silver stain; crescents in ~10%
- **IF**: Critical for classification
  - Immune complex: Ig + C3 along GBM and mesangium
  - C3 glomerulopathy: C3 dominant, no or scant Ig
- **EM**: Subendothelial deposits (immune complex MPGN); intramembranous dense deposits (DDD); both subendothelial + subepithelial (type III, obsolete)

**Exam pearl:** MPGN + low C3 + low C4 = immune complex (classical pathway). MPGN + low C3 + normal C4 = alternative pathway (C3 glomerulopathy). This complement pattern is high-yield.

**Exam pearl:** Drusen on retinal exam may indicate dense deposit disease (C3 glomerulopathy).

### Workup Algorithm

1. Biopsy shows MPGN pattern on LM
2. IF determines category:
   - Ig + C3 present -> immune complex MPGN -> search for secondary cause (HCV, HBV, autoimmune, monoclonal protein, endocarditis)
   - C3 dominant, no Ig -> C3 glomerulopathy -> rule out infection-related GN first -> complement workup (CH50, AP50, C3, C4, factor H, CFH antibodies, genetic testing)
   - If monoclonal Ig deposits -> evaluate for MGRS (SPEP, UPEP, free light chains, hematology)
3. In adults >= 50 years with C3 glomerulopathy, always screen for monoclonal protein

### Treatment

**Immune complex MPGN:**
- Treat underlying cause first (HCV: DAA; HBV: antiviral; autoimmune: disease-specific; MGRS: clone-directed therapy)
- If no underlying cause identified (idiopathic): supportive care with RAAS blockade
- If progressive despite supportive care: corticosteroids +/- immunosuppression based on severity
  - Nephrotic + normal Cr: limited corticosteroid course
  - Active urine sediment + abnormal kidney function: corticosteroids + immunosuppressive therapy
  - Rapidly progressive crescentic GN: pulse methylprednisolone + cyclophosphamide

**C3 glomerulopathy (KDIGO 2021):**
- Supportive care with RAAS blockade
- If moderate-severe (proteinuria > 1 g/day + declining function for >= 6 months): MMF + corticosteroid as first-line
- If no response: eculizumab; if no response to either, consider clinical trial
- **Pegcetacoplan** (Empaveli): expanded FDA approval for primary immune-complex MPGN (>= 12 years old)
- **Iptacopan** (Fabhalta): expanded FDA approval for C3 glomerulopathy in adults

## IgA Nephropathy (IgAN)

### Key Facts

- Most common primary GN worldwide
- Highest prevalence in Southeast Asia (Japan ~30% of biopsies, China ~45%)
- Incidence: 0.06-10.5/100,000 depending on region and biopsy practices
- M:F = 2-6:1; peak incidence 2nd-3rd decade
- 25-30% progress to ESKD over 20-25 years
- Caused by galactose-deficient IgA1 (Gd-IgA1) immune complexes

### Clinical Presentations

| Presentation | Frequency | Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Asymptomatic microscopic hematuria** | Most common | Persistent, detected on screening; proteinuria < 0.5 g/day |
| **Synpharyngitic macroscopic hematuria** | 10-15% | Gross hematuria CONCURRENT with (not 2 weeks after) pharyngitis/URI; age < 40 |
| **Nephrotic syndrome** | Rare | Suggests concomitant MCD or secondary FSGS |
| **RPGN** | Rare | >= 50% GFR decline in <= 3 months; crescentic IgAN |
| **Chronic kidney disease** | Progressive | HTN, proteinuria, declining GFR |

**Exam pearl:** IgAN hematuria occurs WITH the infection (synpharyngitic = simultaneous). Post-streptococcal GN hematuria occurs 1-2 WEEKS AFTER infection. This timing distinction is a classic exam question.

**Exam pearl:** Elevated serum IgA (>= 315 mg/dL in ~50%) and IgA/C3 ratio are NOT sufficient for diagnosis. Kidney biopsy with IgA-dominant mesangial deposits on immunohistology is REQUIRED.

### Diagnosis

- **Biopsy indicated**: proteinuria >= 0.5 g/day (KDIGO)
- **Biopsy may not be needed**: isolated microscopic hematuria + minimal proteinuria (< 0.5 g/day) = unlikely to change management
- **LM**: Mesangial hypercellularity (>= 4 cells/mesangial area), segmental endocapillary proliferation, crescents, segmental sclerosis
- **IF**: **IgA dominant** (or codominant) mesangial deposits; weak IgG and/or IgM may be present
- **EM**: Mesangial and paramesangial electron-dense deposits; occasionally subepithelial and subendothelial

### Oxford (MEST-C) Classification

| Score | Feature | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **M** | Mesangial hypercellularity | <= 50% of glomeruli | > 50% of glomeruli | - |
| **E** | Endocapillary proliferation | Absent | Present | - |
| **S** | Segmental sclerosis/adhesion | Absent | Present | - |
| **T** | Tubular atrophy/interstitial fibrosis | <= 25% | 26-50% | > 50% |
| **C** | Crescents | Absent | < 25% of glomeruli | >= 25% of glomeruli |

### Treatment (KDIGO 2021)

**All patients:**
- ACEi or ARB at maximally tolerated dose [Strong Recommendation]
- Sparsentan [Conditional Recommendation]: dual endothelin/ARB antagonist (do NOT combine with ACEi/ARB)
- SGLT2 inhibitor (dapagliflozin or empagliflozin) in addition to ACEi/ARB/sparsentan [Conditional Recommendation]
- BP target <= 120/70 in adults
- Lifestyle modifications, dietary Na < 2 g/day (90 mmol/day)

**If at risk of progressive loss of kidney function (proteinuria >= 0.5 g/day):**
- Consider glucocorticoids for 6-9 months in addition to supportive therapy [Conditional Recommendation]
- Alternative: targeted-release budesonide (Nefecon) 9-month course [Conditional Recommendation]
  - Only therapy demonstrated to decrease serum Gd-IgA1 and IgA-containing immune complexes
- Estimated GFR <= 30: consider glucocorticoids with increased toxicity risk discussion

**Cytotoxic agents:**
- CYC and MMF may be indicated in crescentic IgAN
- NOT routinely recommended for typical IgAN
- Rituximab, azathioprine, calcineurin inhibitors: evidence does not support use

**Exam pearl:** TESTING-study and STOP-IgAN trial showed steroids reduce proteinuria but increase serious adverse events (infections). Net benefit uncertain. KDIGO 2021 suggests considering steroids if risk of progression is high despite maximal supportive care.

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

1. **Young man with gross hematuria during URI**: IgAN (synpharyngitic). Key: DURING infection, not 2 weeks later. Biopsy if proteinuria >= 0.5 g/day. IF shows mesangial IgA.

2. **Adult with sudden nephrotic syndrome, normal complement, bland sediment**: MCD vs FSGS vs MN. Anti-PLA2R positive = MN (no biopsy needed in typical case). Anti-PLA2R negative + need for biopsy. Normal LM + FPE on EM = MCD. Segmental sclerosis = FSGS.

3. **Black patient with collapsing FSGS and HIV**: HIV-associated nephropathy (HIVAN). APOL1 high-risk genotype. Treatment: ART (not immunosuppression). Collapsing variant = worst prognosis.

4. **Nephrotic syndrome + low C3**: Think MPGN or lupus. Get IF on biopsy: Ig + C3 = immune complex MPGN -> workup for HCV, autoimmune, monoclonal protein. C3 dominant = C3 glomerulopathy -> complement workup.

5. **Middle-aged man with nephrotic syndrome, anti-PLA2R positive**: Primary MN. Risk stratify: if proteinuria > 8 g/day or eGFR declining -> high risk -> rituximab or modified Ponticelli. Monitor PLA2R titers.

6. **Child with nephrotic syndrome, age 3**: MCD (70-90% of pediatric NS). Empiric steroids without biopsy. Biopsy only if: age < 1, age >= 12, steroid-resistant, atypical features (hematuria, low complement, renal failure).

7. **Obese patient with subnephrotic proteinuria, normal albumin**: Secondary (adaptive) FSGS. Treatment: weight loss + ACEi/ARB. NOT immunosuppression. EM shows focal (not diffuse) foot process effacement.

8. **MPGN on biopsy + monotypic Ig deposits**: MGRS (monoclonal gammopathy of renal significance). Must evaluate for underlying hematologic clone even if no overt malignancy. SPEP/UPEP/free light chains/hematology consult.

9. **IgAN patient with proteinuria 1.5 g/day on max ACEi**: Add SGLT2i. If still > 0.5 g/day, consider targeted-release budesonide (Nefecon) or systemic corticosteroids per KDIGO 2021.

10. **MN patient with nephrotic syndrome + renal vein thrombosis**: MN has HIGHEST thrombosis risk among glomerular diseases (albumin < 2.5 g/dL -> prophylactic anticoagulation controversial but considered). Doppler ultrasound for renal vein thrombosis.
