# Magnesium and Phosphate Disorders — Clinical Approach

## Exam Logic Summary

Mg and PO4 disorders are medium-yield but frequently tested as part of CKD-MBD, refeeding syndrome, and electrolyte panel interpretation. The exam tests: (1) identifying the cause from clinical context, (2) associated electrolyte derangements (especially hypoK and hypoCa with hypoMg), (3) refeeding syndrome recognition, (4) phosphate binder selection in CKD, (5) ECG changes. Common distractor: treating hypokalemia without correcting concurrent hypomagnesemia.

**Textbook ref:** Brenner Ch. 19 (Disorders of Calcium, Magnesium, and Phosphate Balance), Daugirdas Ch. 36 (Bone Disease), Harrison Ch. 309 (Bone and Mineral Metabolism)

## Part 1: Magnesium Disorders

### Normal Physiology

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Normal serum Mg | 1.8-2.4 mg/dL (0.75-1.0 mmol/L) |
| Total body Mg | ~25 g (1,000 mmol) in adult |
| Distribution | 60% bone, 20% muscle, 20% soft tissue; ~1% in blood |
| Serum fractions | ionized (free), protein-bound (~30%), complexed with anions |
| Daily intake | >= 310 mg (women), >= 400 mg (men) |
| GI absorption | 24-76% in small intestine (paracellular + TRPM6/TRPM7) |
| Renal handling | ~2,400 mg filtered daily; 95% reabsorbed |
| Major reabsorption site | Thick ascending limb (TAL) 60-70%, NOT proximal tubule (only 10%) |
| Distal tubule | 5-10% fine-tuning via transcellular TRPM6 |
| Urinary excretion | 3-5% (~100 mg/day); adjustable 0.5-70% of filtered load |

**Exam pearl:** Unlike most ions, the MAJOR reabsorption site for Mg is the TAL (not PCT). This is why loop diuretics cause Mg wasting but thiazides cause LESS Mg wasting.

### Mg Functions (cofactor for >300 enzymes)

- Na/K-ATPase function (critical for K homeostasis)
- Neuromuscular transmission
- Cardiac conduction and repolarization
- PTH secretion regulation
- Insulin signaling
- Bone formation

### Hypermagnesemia

**Definition:** Serum Mg > 2.6 mg/dL (> 1.1 mmol/L). Symptomatic usually > 4.8 mg/dL (> 2.0 mmol/L).

#### Causes Table

| Category | Mechanism | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Excess intake | Overwhelms renal excretion | Mg-containing laxatives/cathartics, antacids, enemas, IV MgSO4 (eclampsia), Dead Sea water ingestion |
| Decreased excretion | Reduced GFR | AKI, CKD stages 4-5, familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia |
| Redistribution | ICF → ECF shift | Metabolic acidosis, tumor lysis syndrome |
| Endocrine | Reduced excretion | Adrenal insufficiency, hypothyroidism |

**Exam pearl:** Symptomatic hyperMg is rare with normal kidney function. The classic scenario = elderly patient with CKD taking Mg-containing laxatives/antacids.

#### Clinical Manifestations by Serum Level

| Serum Mg (mg/dL) | Manifestation |
|---|---|
| < 4.8 | Usually asymptomatic |
| 4.8-7.2 | Nausea, flushing, headache, lethargy, ↓ DTR |
| 7.2-12 | Somnolence, ↓↓ DTR, hypotension, bradycardia |
| > 12 | Absent DTR, flaccid paralysis, respiratory depression |
| > 15 | Complete heart block, cardiac arrest |

**Exam pearl:** Loss of deep tendon reflexes is the earliest reliable sign. Monitor DTR in patients receiving IV MgSO4 (e.g., eclampsia) — loss of patellar reflex = stop infusion.

#### ECG Changes in Hypermagnesemia

- Prolonged PR interval
- Widened QRS
- Prolonged QT
- Complete heart block
- Asystole (extreme)

#### Diagnostic Workup

- Serum Mg, Ca, K, BUN, creatinine
- PTH, vitamin D, phosphate (assess concurrent Ca abnormalities)
- Muscle enzymes (if rhabdomyolysis suspected)
- ECG if bradycardia or tachycardia
- Urine studies NOT typically helpful (renal failure is usually present)

#### Management

| Severity | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Mild, normal renal function | Stop Mg-containing agents, IV saline for volume |
| Moderate + some renal function | Above + loop diuretics (enhance Mg excretion); monitor Ca |
| Cardiac/respiratory symptoms | IV calcium gluconate 1 g over 3 min OR CaCl2 500-1000 mg IV (antagonizes Mg at NMJ and cardiac channels) |
| Severe + renal failure | Dialysis |

**Exam pearl:** IV calcium is the ANTIDOTE for severe hyperMg — it directly antagonizes Mg at the neuromuscular junction and cardiac calcium channels. Same concept as Ca for hyperK.

### Hypomagnesemia

**Definition:** Serum Mg < 1.8 mg/dL (< 0.75 mmol/L). Lower limit may be < 1.5 mg/dL (< 0.62 mmol/L). Symptomatic usually < 1.2 mg/dL (< 0.5 mmol/L).

**Prevalence:** 2.5-15% general population; up to 65% ICU patients; 12% hospitalized; 30% alcoholics.

#### Causes Table

| Category | Mechanism | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Redistribution (ICF shift)** | Mg moves intracellularly | Refeeding syndrome, insulin/glucose infusion, DKA treatment, hungry bone syndrome, correction of acidosis, acute pancreatitis (saponification) |
| **Decreased GI absorption** | Reduced intake or impaired absorption | Alcoholism (30% prevalence), malnutrition, anorexia nervosa, diarrhea (more than vomiting), short bowel, malabsorption, PPI use (TRPM6/7 inhibition) |
| **Increased renal wasting** | Renal Mg loss | Loop diuretics, thiazides, cisplatin, aminoglycosides, amphotericin B, calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, cyclosporine), cetuximab (EGFR inhibitor), foscarnet, digoxin |
| **Endocrine** | Various mechanisms | DM types 1 and 2, hypercalcemia (competes at TAL), hyperthyroidism, hyperaldosteronism |
| **Genetic** | Tubular defects | Gitelman syndrome, Bartter type 4, EAST syndrome, FHHNC, familial hypoMg |
| **Other** | Mixed | ECF expansion, ATN recovery, cardiopulmonary bypass, burns, exercise/heat |

**Exam pearl (PPI + hypoMg):** FDA warns of hypoMg with long-term PPI use (inhibits TRPM6/7 in intestine). Often with concurrent hypoCa. Resolves on PPI withdrawal. Check baseline Mg before starting long-term PPI.

#### Associated Electrolyte Derangements (HIGH YIELD)

| Derangement | Mechanism | Clinical significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Hypokalemia** (40-60% of cases) | (1) Common causes deplete both; (2) HypoMg opens ROMK channels → K wasting | **Refractory hypokalemia until Mg is corrected** |
| **Hypocalcemia** | (1) Impaired PTH secretion; (2) End-organ resistance to PTH; (3) Reduced 1,25(OH)2D production | **Refractory hypocalcemia until Mg is corrected** |

**Exam pearl:** If a patient has refractory hypokalemia or hypocalcemia that does not respond to supplementation, ALWAYS check and correct Mg first. This is a classic board question.

#### Clinical Manifestations

- **Neuromuscular:** hyperexcitability, tremor, tetany, Chvostek/Trousseau signs, muscle fasciculations, cramps, vertical nystagmus, seizures
- **Cardiac:** atrial and ventricular arrhythmias, prolonged QT, torsades de pointes, digitalis toxicity potentiation
- **CNS:** depression, agitation, psychosis, delirium, apathy, coma
- **Other:** chondrocalcinosis (chronic)

#### ECG Changes in Hypomagnesemia

- Flattened T waves and U waves
- Prolonged QT interval
- Widened QRS (severe)
- Prolonged PR interval (severe)
- Torsades de pointes

**Exam pearl:** HypoMg ECG changes overlap with hypoK. Both prolong QT → torsades risk. Combined hypoMg + hypoK = highest arrhythmia risk.

#### Diagnostic Workup

1. Serum Mg (< 1% of total body Mg — may underestimate deficiency)
2. Concurrent: serum K, Ca, PO4, BUN/Cr
3. **FEMg on spot urine** to differentiate renal vs GI loss:

| Test | Renal wasting | GI loss / reduced intake |
|---|---|---|
| FEMg | > 2% | < 2% |
| 24h urine Mg | > 24 mg (> 1 mmol) | < 12 mg (< 0.5 mmol) |

4. ECG in patients with arrhythmias or concurrent hypoK
5. Genetic testing if inherited (family history, unexplained, infancy onset)

#### Treatment

**Asymptomatic / mild:**
- Oral magnesium supplements, 2-4 tablets daily
- Sustained-release preferred (less diarrhea): MgCl (64 mg elem), Mg-L-lactate (84 mg elem)
- Standard preparations: MgO (242 mg elem/400 mg tab, 61%), Mg gluconate (27 mg/500 mg tab, 5%)

**Severe or symptomatic:**
- IV MgSO4: 1 g = 100 mg (4 mmol) elemental Mg
- Day 1: 8-12 g IV MgSO4
- Days 2-4: 4-6 g/day IV
- Rate: do not exceed 2 g/hour (rapid infusion → renal excretion)
- Target: serum Mg > 1 mg/dL (> 0.4 mmol/L)
- **Renal insufficiency: give 25-50% of dose**
- Monitor ECG in elderly/high-dose patients

**Concurrent hypocalcemia:** treat Ca BEFORE Mg replacement (MgSO4 sulfate complexes with ionized Ca → increased urinary Ca excretion).

**Chronic renal Mg wasting on diuretics:** add K-sparing diuretic (amiloride, triamterene) — blocks ENaC, indirectly reduces Mg wasting.

## Part 2: Phosphate Disorders

### Normal Physiology

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Normal serum PO4 (adults) | 2.5-4.5 mg/dL (0.8-1.5 mmol/L) |
| Body distribution | 85% bone/teeth, 15% soft tissue + ECF |
| Serum forms | H2PO4- and HPO42- (ratio 1:4 at pH 7.4); 10-15% protein-bound |
| Daily intake | 800-1,500 mg; ~70% absorbed in small intestine |
| Renal handling | Filtered at glomerulus, reabsorbed in PCT (NPT2a, NPT2c cotransporters) |
| Regulation | PTH (↑ excretion via NPT2a internalization), FGF-23 (↑ excretion, ↓ 1,25(OH)2D), 1,25(OH)2D (↑ gut absorption) |
| TmP/GFR | Maximum tubular phosphate reabsorption per GFR; < 2.5 mg/dL = renal phosphate wasting |

**Exam pearl:** Serum PO4 does NOT reflect total body stores (most is intracellular/bone). A patient with normal serum PO4 can be severely phosphate-depleted (e.g., DKA before insulin).

### Hyperphosphatemia

**Definition:** Serum PO4 > 4.5 mg/dL (> 1.45 mmol/L) in adults.

**Key fact:** Persistent hyperphosphatemia is rare without renal failure or hypoparathyroidism.

#### Causes Table

| Category | Mechanism | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Decreased renal excretion** | ↓ GFR | CKD stages 3-5 (MOST COMMON chronic cause), AKI |
| | ↑ Proximal reabsorption | Hypoparathyroidism, pseudohypoparathyroidism, acromegaly, vitamin D toxicity |
| | Genetic | Familial tumoral calcinosis (↓ FGF-23 or FGF-23 resistance) |
| **Increased load** | Exogenous | PO4-containing laxatives/enemas, IV phosphate, bowel prep solutions |
| **Redistribution (ICF → ECF)** | Cell lysis/shift | Tumor lysis syndrome (TLS), rhabdomyolysis, hemolytic anemia, metabolic/respiratory acidosis, DKA |
| **Pseudohyperphosphatemia** | Lab artifact | Hyperglobulinemia (myeloma), hyperlipidemia, hyperbilirubinemia, high-dose liposomal amphotericin B, heparinized samples |

**Exam pearl:** HyperPO4 + hyperK + hyperuricemia + ↑LDH + hypoCa = tumor lysis syndrome. If + AKI → urgent dialysis indication.

#### Clinical Manifestations

- Usually asymptomatic unless concurrent hypocalcemia
- Acute: symptoms from hypocalcemia (muscle cramps, tetany, perioral numbness, seizures, QTc prolongation)
- Chronic (CKD): vascular calcification → CV mortality, secondary hyperparathyroidism, renal osteodystrophy, calciphylaxis

#### PTH-Based Diagnostic Algorithm

| PTH | Calcium | Likely diagnosis |
|---|---|---|
| High | Normal/low | CKD, pseudohypoparathyroidism |
| Low | Low/normal | Hypoparathyroidism |
| Normal | Normal | Tumoral calcinosis, hyperostosis |

#### Management — Acute

- Self-resolving if normal kidney function
- Treat underlying cause (acidosis correction, DKA insulin)
- Acute exogenous overload + intact renal function: stop PO4 supplements, hydration, monitor Ca
- Acute severe + renal failure (esp TLS with K >= 7): urgent dialysis

#### Management — Chronic (CKD) — HIGH YIELD

**KDIGO 2017 CKD-MBD Guideline recommendations:**

| Strategy | Details | KDIGO Grade |
|---|---|---|
| **Dietary restriction** | < 800-1,000 mg phosphorus/day; consider source (processed foods = high bioavailability) | 2D |
| **Phosphate binders** | Take WITH meals; all types similarly efficacious | See below |
| **Dialysis** | Standard HD removes ~800 mg/session (3x/week) | -- |
| **Target PO4** | Lower toward normal range in CKD 3-5 | 2C |
| **Restrict Ca-based binders** | In adults on phosphate-lowering therapy | 2B |
| **Avoid aluminum binders** | Long-term use → aluminum intoxication; avoid dialysate Al contamination | 1C |
| **Dialysate Ca** | Maintain 1.25-1.5 mmol/L (2.5-3 mEq/L) | 2C |

#### Phosphate Binders Comparison (EXAM TABLE)

| Binder | Mechanism | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sevelamer** (Renagel/Renvela) | Non-Ca, non-Al polymer | No Ca load, ↓ LDL, no Al toxicity | GI side effects, pill burden, cost; ↓ bioavailability of some drugs |
| **Lanthanum carbonate** (Fosrenol) | Non-Ca rare earth metal | Chewable, low pill burden | Nausea, radio-opaque on X-ray |
| **Ferric citrate** (Auryxia) | Iron-based binder + Fe supplement | Dual benefit (PO4 + iron repletion) | Diarrhea, discolored feces; CI in iron overload |
| **Sucroferric oxyhydroxide** (Velphoro) | Iron-based | Low pill burden, chewable | Discolored feces, diarrhea |
| **Calcium acetate** (PhosLo) | Ca-based; binds PO4 in gut | Cheap, effective | Hypercalcemia risk, vascular calcification concern; CI if hypercalcemic |
| **Calcium carbonate** | Ca-based | Cheapest | Needs stomach acid, less effective at high pH; hypercalcemia; constipation |

**Exam pearl:** KDIGO suggests restricting calcium-based binder dose (2B). Non-Ca binders preferred in patients with hypercalcemia, vascular calcification, adynamic bone disease, or persistently low PTH.

#### Sevelamer Dosing (by serum PO4)

| Serum PO4 | Sevelamer carbonate dose |
|---|---|
| 5.5-7.5 mg/dL | 800 mg TID with meals |
| 7.5-9 mg/dL | 1.6 g TID |
| >= 9 mg/dL | 1.6 g TID |

#### Prognosis Data

- Serum PO4 > 5.2 mg/dL in HD patients → increased mortality (COSMOS study, NDT 2015; N=6,307; safest range 3.6-5.2 mg/dL)
- Each 1 mg/dL increase in serum PO4 → HR 1.33 for all-cause mortality in CKD non-dialysis (JASN 2005)
- > 50% of mortality in ESKD = cardiovascular disease, heavily linked to vascular calcification from hyperPO4

### Hypophosphatemia

**Definition:** Serum PO4 < 2.5 mg/dL (< 0.8 mmol/L).

| Severity | Serum PO4 |
|---|---|
| Mild | 2.0-2.5 mg/dL (0.65-0.81 mmol/L) |
| Moderate | 1.0-2.0 mg/dL (0.32-0.65 mmol/L) |
| Severe | < 1.0 mg/dL (< 0.32 mmol/L) |

**Prevalence:** 28-34% ICU patients; 65-80% sepsis; up to 30% alcoholics; 0.2-2.2% hospitalized.

#### Causes Table

| Category | Mechanism | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Redistribution (ECF → ICF)** | PO4 shifts into cells | **Refeeding syndrome** (most classic), insulin/glucose infusion, respiratory alkalosis (PCO2 < 20 → PO4 < 1), DKA treatment, hungry bone syndrome, rapid cell proliferation (AML, lymphoma) |
| **Decreased GI absorption** | Reduced intake/absorption | Malnutrition, alcoholism, vitamin D deficiency, malabsorption (Crohn, celiac), phosphate binders (sevelamer, Ca/Al/Mg antacids), chronic diarrhea |
| **Increased renal excretion** | Renal PO4 wasting | **FGF-23 mediated:** XLH, tumor-induced osteomalacia, ADHR, ARHR, fibrous dysplasia. **Non-FGF-23:** hyperparathyroidism, Fanconi syndrome, post-kidney transplant, diuretics (acetazolamide, thiazides, loops), IV iron (ferric carboxymaltose: 0-92% incidence!) |
| **Multiple mechanisms** | Combined | Alcoholism (intake + redistribution + renal), DKA (osmotic diuresis + insulin treatment), sepsis |

#### Refeeding Syndrome (EXAM FAVORITE)

**Definition:** Metabolic derangements occurring when nutrition is reintroduced after prolonged starvation.

**Pathophysiology:** Starvation depletes intracellular PO4/K/Mg stores while serum levels remain normal. Carbohydrate intake → insulin surge → drives PO4/K/Mg intracellularly → acute drops in serum levels.

**Classic triad:** HypoPO4 + hypoK + hypoMg (all shift intracellularly)

**At-risk:** anorexia nervosa, alcoholism, prolonged fasting, cancer cachexia, postoperative TPN initiation

**Prevention:** Start feeding slowly (10-20 kcal/kg/day), supplement PO4/K/Mg prophylactically, monitor electrolytes daily for 5-7 days.

#### Clinical Manifestations (mostly at PO4 < 1-2 mg/dL)

- **Musculoskeletal:** weakness, myalgia, rhabdomyolysis (acute, not chronic hypoPO4), osteomalacia
- **Respiratory:** diaphragmatic weakness → respiratory failure, difficulty weaning from ventilator
- **Cardiac:** LV dysfunction, cardiomyopathy, arrhythmias
- **Hematologic:** hemolysis (↓ RBC 2,3-DPG and ATP), leukocyte dysfunction
- **Neurologic:** paresthesias, confusion, seizures, coma, paralysis

**Exam pearl:** Acute severe hypoPO4 in ICU patient on ventilator + unable to wean → think phosphate depletion (diaphragmatic weakness).

#### Diagnostic Workup

1. Serum PO4, Ca, albumin, Cr, PTH, 25(OH)D, 1,25(OH)2D, FGF-23, ALP
2. ABG (respiratory alkalosis?)
3. Urine PO4 and creatinine:
   - **Fractional excretion of phosphorus = (urine PO4 x plasma Cr x 100) / (plasma PO4 x urine Cr)**
   - FEphos > 5% = renal phosphate wasting
   - FEphos < 5% = intracellular shift or GI losses
4. TmP/GFR = serum PO4 - (urine PO4 x serum Cr / urine Cr); < 2.5 mg/dL = renal wasting
5. If renal wasting present: check PTH, FGF-23, Ca to differentiate causes

#### Treatment

| Severity | Route | Regimen |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild symptomatic** (2-2.5 mg/dL) | Oral | 1,000-2,000 mg/day PO4 in 3-4 divided doses, 7-10 days |
| **Moderate, non-ventilated** (1-2 mg/dL) | Oral | 1,000-2,000 mg/day (32-64 mmol/day) |
| **Moderate, ventilated** | IV | 2.5-5 mg/kg (0.08-0.16 mmol/kg) over 2-6 hours |
| **Severe asymptomatic** (< 1 mg/dL) | IV | 2.5 mg/kg (0.08 mmol/kg) over 6 hours |
| **Severe symptomatic** | IV | 5 mg/kg (0.16 mmol/kg) over 6 hours |
| **Severe, intensive** | IV | 10 mg/kg (0.32 mmol/kg) over 12 hours |

**Graded ICU dosing (no renal failure, no hypercalcemia):**

| Serum PO4 | IV phosphate dose |
|---|---|
| 2.3-3 mg/dL | 5 mg/kg over 4-6 hours |
| 1.6-2.2 mg/dL | 10 mg/kg over 4-6 hours |
| < 1.5 mg/dL | 20 mg/kg over 6-8 hours |

**IV phosphate cautions:**
- Contraindicated in: renal failure, hypercalcemia, hyperkalemia (if using K-phosphate)
- Risks of aggressive IV: hyperPO4, hypotension, hypocalcemia (tetany), metastatic calcification
- Discontinue IV when PO4 > 2 mg/dL, switch to oral
- Use Na-phosphate if hyperK; K-phosphate if hypoK
- Cow's milk = 230 mg PO4 per 8 oz (oral alternative)

## Mg-PO4 Interactions and Cross-Links

| Interaction | Mechanism | Clinical relevance |
|---|---|---|
| HypoMg causes hypoPO4 | HypoMg → impaired PTH secretion → ↓ PTH → theoretically less PO4 excretion; but main link is shared causes (refeeding, alcoholism) | Check both in refeeding syndrome |
| HyperPO4 + hypoCa in CKD | PO4 binds Ca → precipitation + ↓ 1,25(OH)2D + ↑ FGF-23 + 2ndary hyperPTH | Core CKD-MBD pathophysiology |
| Mg-containing antacids cause hypoPO4 | Mg binds dietary PO4 in gut → reduced absorption | Common in elderly on OTC antacids |
| Both depleted in refeeding | Insulin-driven intracellular shift | Replace PO4 + Mg + K simultaneously |

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

1. **Refractory hypokalemia:** 55F on thiazide + PPI presents with K 2.8 despite aggressive KCl. Mg 1.0. Answer: Correct hypoMg first (Mg needed for ROMK channel closure; without Mg, K continues to be wasted renally).

2. **Refeeding syndrome:** 30F with anorexia nervosa admitted, TPN started. Day 2: PO4 0.5, K 2.5, Mg 1.1, new arrhythmia. Answer: Refeeding syndrome — acute intracellular shift of PO4/K/Mg driven by insulin. Stop aggressive feeding, supplement all three, restart nutrition slowly.

3. **CKD-MBD phosphate binder selection:** 62M on HD, PO4 7.8, Ca 10.8 (high-normal), PTH 800. Which binder? Answer: Non-calcium binder (sevelamer or lanthanum) — Ca already high-normal, risk of vascular calcification. KDIGO 2B: restrict Ca-based binders.

4. **PPI-induced hypomagnesemia:** 70F on omeprazole 5 years, presents with tetany, Mg 0.8, Ca 7.2, K 3.0. Answer: PPI-induced hypoMg → secondary hypoCa (impaired PTH secretion + PTH resistance) + hypoK. Stop PPI, IV MgSO4, treat Ca before Mg.

5. **Tumor lysis syndrome:** 45M with Burkitt lymphoma, day 2 of chemo: PO4 9.5, K 6.8, uric acid 15, Ca 6.2, Cr rising. Answer: TLS — hyperPO4 + hyperK + hyperuricemia + hypoCa + AKI. Urgent dialysis if K >= 7 or refractory. Rasburicase for uric acid.
