# Pediatric Nephrology — Board Review

## Exam Logic Summary

Pediatric nephrology is high-yield for TSN. The exam tests: (1) age-specific AKI definitions (pRIFLE vs KDIGO), (2) etiology differences from adults (CAKUT #1 cause of CKD in children vs DM in adults), (3) steroid-sensitive vs steroid-resistant nephrotic syndrome management, (4) nephronophthisis as #1 genetic cause of ESKD in children, (5) RTA classification in children with emphasis on genetic causes. Common distractor: applying adult AKI criteria to neonates.

## AKI in Children

### Definitions — Three Classification Systems

| System | Criteria basis | Key features |
|---|---|---|
| **pRIFLE** | Estimated CrCl (Schwartz) + UOP | Risk/Injury/Failure/Loss/End-stage; uses % decrease in eCrCl |
| **AKIN** | sCr increase + UOP | 3 stages; mainly for older adolescents/adults |
| **KDIGO** | sCr increase or UOP | Preferred current standard; applies to children and adults |

### KDIGO AKI Staging (Children and Adults)

| Stage | Serum creatinine | Urine output |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | sCr increase >= 0.3 mg/dL within 48h OR 1.5-1.9x baseline within 7d | < 0.5 mL/kg/h for 6-12h |
| 2 | sCr 2.0-2.9x baseline | < 0.5 mL/kg/h for >= 12h |
| 3 | sCr >= 3x baseline OR >= 4 mg/dL OR initiation of RRT | < 0.3 mL/kg/h for >= 24h OR anuria >= 12h |

**Exam pearl:** In children < 18 years, decrease in eGFR to < 35 mL/min/1.73 m2 also qualifies as stage 3.

### pRIFLE Classification (Pediatric-Specific)

| Stage | eCrCl decrease | Urine output |
|---|---|---|
| **R**isk | 25% | < 0.5 mL/kg/h x 8h |
| **I**njury | 50% | < 0.5 mL/kg/h x 12h |
| **F**ailure | 75% OR GFR < 35 | < 0.5 mL/kg/h x 24h OR anuria x 12h |
| **L**oss | Failure > 4 weeks | - |
| **E**SKD | Failure > 3 months | - |

### Neonatal AKI

- No standardized definition; proposed neonatal KDIGO modification for infants < 120 days
- Stage 1: sCr increase >= 0.3 mg/dL within 48h OR 1.5-1.9x reference within 7d OR UOP < 0.5 mL/kg/h x 6-12h
- Stage 3: sCr >= 3x reference OR >= 2.5 mg/dL OR RRT initiation OR UOP < 0.3 mL/kg/h x 24h OR anuria x 12h
- **Exam pearl:** Neonatal sCr reflects MATERNAL creatinine in first few days of life (placental transfer). Must wait 48-72h for sCr to reflect neonatal renal function.

### Epidemiology

- AKI incidence: 3.9/1,000 hospital admissions in children
- ICU AKI prevalence: 27-30% in pediatric ICU
- Neonatal ICU: early-onset AKI 21%, late-onset AKI 9%

### Etiology — Age-Dependent

**Children:**

| Category | Causes | Key feature |
|---|---|---|
| Prerenal (most common) | Volume depletion, sepsis, heart failure | FENa < 1%, BUN/Cr > 20 |
| Intrinsic | ATN, GN, HUS, interstitial nephritis | FENa > 2% (ATN) |
| Postrenal | Calculi, tumor, posterior urethral valves | Bilateral obstruction required |

**Neonates (distinct pattern):**
- Prerenal (85%): decreased renal blood flow (placental abruption, GI losses, high insensible losses in premature)
- Intrinsic (11%): ATN, nephrotoxic medications (acyclovir, aminoglycosides, amphotericin B, vancomycin)
- Postrenal (3%): posterior urethral valves, prune belly syndrome, UPJ obstruction

**Exam pearl:** Posterior urethral valves = most important obstructive cause of AKI in male neonates. Suspect if prenatal US shows hydronephrosis + enlarged bladder + oligohydramnios.

### Renal Development Facts (Exam-Relevant)

- Nephrogenesis: begins week 5, continues until 34-36 weeks gestation
- Term neonates: full nephron complement (200,000-2.7 million) but only 25% of adult GFR
- Neonatal GFR: 10-20 mL/min/1.73 m2 at birth; 30-40 at 2 weeks; mature GFR by age 2
- Cardiac output to kidneys: 2.5-4% at birth → 6% at 24h → 15-18% at 6 weeks → 20-25% in adults
- **Exam pearl:** NSAIDs and ACEi are contraindicated in neonates because prostaglandin-mediated afferent arteriolar dilation and angiotensin II-mediated efferent constriction are critical for maintaining GFR in immature kidneys.

### Management Pearls

- Fluid resuscitation first for prerenal AKI
- ECG if K+ >= 5.5 mEq/L (5.5 mmol/L) or tumor lysis syndrome or rhabdomyolysis
- Diuretics for volume overload; fenoldopam may improve renal function in critically ill
- RRT when life-threatening fluid, electrolyte, or acid-base imbalance
- Refer to pediatric nephrologist: intrinsic AKI, persistent proteinuria, required dialysis

## CKD in Children

### Definition (KDIGO)

CKD = abnormalities of kidney structure or function present for > 3 months with health implications.

**For children >= 2 years:** same GFR categories as adults (G1-G5)
**For children < 2 years:** normal GFR <= 1 SD below mean for age; moderately reduced > 1 SD below mean; severely reduced > 2 SD below mean

### Age-Appropriate Normal GFR Values (MUST KNOW)

| Age | Mean GFR (mL/min/1.73 m2) | SD |
|---|---|---|
| Neonates 1 week | 41 | 15 |
| Infants 2-8 weeks | 66 | 25 |
| Infants > 8 weeks | 96 | 22 |
| Children 2-12 years | 133 | 27 |
| Adolescents 13-21 (M) | 140 | 30 |
| Adolescents 13-21 (F) | 126 | 22 |

**Exam pearl:** A sCr of 0.8 mg/dL in a 2-year-old = significant kidney disease, whereas in an adult it is normal. Always interpret sCr relative to age, height, and muscle mass.

### GFR Estimation in Children

- **Updated bedside Schwartz (CKiDCR):** eGFR = 41.3 x (height[m] / sCr[mg/dL])
- **CKiD U25CR equation:** eGFR = K x (height[m] / sCr); K varies by age and sex
- **EKFC equation:** continuous estimation for age >= 2 years through adulthood
- Cystatin C-based equations preferred when muscle mass is low (CKD patients with poor growth)

**Exam pearl:** CKD-EPI40 equation with age-adjusted creatinine shows similar accuracy to Schwartz bedside in children 2-17 years (Kidney Int 2021).

### Proteinuria Categories in Children (KDIGO)

| Category | PCR | ACR | PER | AER |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A1 (normal-mild) | < 150 mg/g | < 30 mg/g | < 150 mg/24h | < 30 mg/24h |
| A2 (moderate) | 150-500 mg/g | 30-300 mg/g | 150-500 mg/24h | 30-300 mg/24h |
| A3 (severe/nephrotic) | > 500 mg/g | > 300 mg/g | > 500 mg/24h | > 300 mg/24h |

Note: Proteinuria (not albuminuria) may replace ACR for staging in children.

### Etiology of CKD in Children (vs Adults)

| Cause | Children | Adults |
|---|---|---|
| #1 | **CAKUT** (45-48%): hypoplasia, dysplasia, obstructive uropathy | Diabetes (DKD) |
| #2 | Glomerular disease (31-34%): FSGS, GN | Hypertension |
| #3 | Cystic/hereditary/congenital (12%) | Glomerular disease |

**Exam pearl:** CAKUT predominates in younger children; glomerular disease becomes leading cause in children >= 12 years. FSGS is most common in Black patients (22.6%).

### Risk Factors (Exam Relevant)

- Family history of genetic kidney disease (ADPKD, ARPKD)
- CAKUT
- **Low birth weight:** OR 1.58 for ESKD, OR 1.79 for low eGFR (Am J Kidney Dis 2009)
- **Preterm birth < 37 weeks:** HR 1.94 for CKD from birth to midadulthood (BMJ 2019)
- **Preterm < 28 weeks:** HR 3.01 for CKD
- Prior AKI, HUS, acute nephritis, nephrotic syndrome
- Single functioning kidney: increased risk of HTN, proteinuria, CKD progression

### Management Principles

- **BP target:** ABPM <= 50th percentile for age/sex/height (Conditional Recommendation)
- ACEi/ARB: for CKD with moderate-severe albuminuria (A2-A3) WITHOUT diabetes; also WITH diabetes
- Avoid nephrotoxic drugs (NSAIDs)
- **Growth:** recombinant human growth hormone for CKD stages 2-5D with height deficits after malnutrition corrected
- **Anemia:** treat correctable causes (iron deficiency, inflammation) before ESAs
- **CKD-MBD:** monitor Ca, P, PTH, ALP starting at CKD stage 2; treat metabolic acidosis if HCO3- < 22
- **Hyperkalemia threshold:** > 5.5 mEq/L in children; > 6 mEq/L in neonates (higher K+ may be normal in premature)
- Kidney transplant preferred over dialysis for ESKD (4-fold survival advantage)
- Transition planning: pediatric to adult care at age 11-14 years

## Nephrotic Syndrome in Children

### Definitions (IPNA/KDIGO)

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Nephrotic-range proteinuria | UPCR >= 2 g/g (200 mg/mmol) OR dipstick >= 3+ |
| Nephrotic syndrome | Nephrotic proteinuria + hypoalbuminemia (< 30 g/L / 3 g/dL) + edema |
| Complete remission | Proteinuria absent x >= 3 consecutive days: UPCR <= 200 mg/g OR dipstick < 1+ |
| Partial remission | UPCR 200-2000 mg/g AND albumin >= 30 g/L |
| Relapse | Dipstick >= 3+ on 3 consecutive days OR UPCR >= 200 mg/mmol on first morning sample |
| SSNS | Complete remission within 4 weeks of standard-dose corticosteroid |
| SRNS | No complete remission after 4 weeks of daily corticosteroid at standard dose |
| Infrequent relapse | 1 relapse within 6 months OR 1-3 relapses in any 12-month period |
| Frequent relapse | >= 2 relapses within first 6 months OR >= 4 in any 12-month period |
| SDNS | 2 consecutive relapses during or within 15 days of stopping corticosteroid |

### Epidemiology

- Prevalence: 1.15-16.9 per 100,000 children; incidence ~4.7 per 100,000
- Male:female = 2:1 (predominance does NOT persist in adolescence)
- Peak age: school-aged children

### Etiology

**Primary (idiopathic) causes:**
- **Minimal change disease (77%)** — most common cause of SSNS
- **FSGS** — most common cause of SRNS
- MPGN, membranous (uncommon in children; when present, suspect underlying disease)
- IgM nephropathy, C1q nephropathy

**Exam pearl:** In children 1-12 years with typical presentation (periorbital edema, nephrotic proteinuria, no hematuria) → assume MCD. No biopsy needed. Treat empirically with steroids.

**Biopsy indications at onset:**
- Age < 1 year (genetic testing first) OR age > 12 years
- Gross hematuria, persistent microscopic hematuria
- Low C3
- Persistent hypertension
- Renal failure unrelated to hypovolemia
- Suspected secondary cause

**Biopsy indications during treatment:**
- Steroid-resistant nephrotic syndrome
- Before calcineurin inhibitor therapy planned

### Pathogenesis — Edema (Two Hypotheses)

| Model | Mechanism | Volume status |
|---|---|---|
| **Underfill** | ↓ albumin → ↓ oncotic pressure → fluid to interstitium → ↓ intravascular volume → RAAS activation → Na retention | Hypovolemia |
| **Overfill** | Proteinuria → primary renal Na retention (ENaC activation in distal tubule) → volume expansion → edema | Hypervolemia |

**Exam pearl:** Underfill vs overfill determines management: underfill = albumin + diuretics cautiously; overfill = diuretics alone safe. Signs of hypovolemia (tachycardia, prolonged cap refill, AKI) = underfill.

### Complications

- **Thrombosis:** ↓ antithrombin (40-80% have reduced levels), ↑ factor V/VIII/fibrinogen, ↑ platelet aggregation. DVT > arterial events.
- **Infection:** ↓ IgG (urinary losses), T-cell dysfunction. Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis = classic (encapsulated organisms). Vaccination: pneumococcal, meningococcal, varicella, influenza.
- **Hyperlipidemia:** ↑ lipoprotein synthesis responding to ↓ albumin; ↑ triglycerides from ↓ lipoprotein lipase (angiopoietin-like 4)
- **Hypothyroidism:** loss of thyroid-binding globulin
- **Vitamin D deficiency:** loss of vitamin D-binding protein
- **Hypocalcemia:** ↓ protein-bound Ca (ionized Ca may be normal)

### Management — Initial Episode (KDIGO Level 1 Grade B / IPNA Strong Rec Level A)

**First episode, age 3 months to 18 years, idiopathic NS:**

| Duration | Daily phase | Alternate-day phase |
|---|---|---|
| 8-week course | Prednisone 60 mg/m2/day or 2 mg/kg/day (max 60 mg) x 4 weeks | 40 mg/m2 or 1.5 mg/kg (max 50 mg) alternate days x 4 weeks |
| 12-week course | Same x 6 weeks | Same x 6 weeks |

- Single morning dose (IPNA Moderate Rec Level B)
- Tapered dosing NOT recommended during alternate-day phase (IPNA Strong Rec Level A)
- No additional immunosuppressive drugs for initial episode (IPNA Weak Rec Level C)

**Evidence on duration:** Cochrane 2024 (54 RCTs, 4,670 children): 5-7 months corticosteroid associated with decreased relapse at 12-24 months vs 3 months (RR 0.64, 95% CI 0.5-0.81, NNT 3-8). But longer treatment did NOT reduce frequent relapses.

### Management — Relapse

**Infrequent relapse (SSNS):**
- Prednisone 60 mg/m2/day until remission x >= 3 days, then 40 mg/m2 alternate days x 4 weeks

**Frequent relapse / SDNS:**
- First-line: prednisone until remission (IPNA Moderate Rec Level B)
- Maintenance: low-dose prednisone daily or alternate days (IPNA Strong Rec Level A)
- If uncontrolled or complicated → corticosteroid-sparing agent (IPNA Strong Rec Level A)

**Corticosteroid-sparing agents (choose based on scenario + patient preference):**
1. Mycophenolate mofetil
2. Calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine, tacrolimus)
3. Rituximab
4. Cyclophosphamide
5. Levamisole (not available in US)

**Exam pearl:** Approach = start with low-dose alternate-day prednisone. If relapses continue → MMF. If MMF fails → CNI (cyclosporine/tacrolimus). Cyclophosphamide = last resort (malignancy risk). Rituximab increasingly used earlier.

### SRNS Management

- Requires specialized pediatric nephrology center
- Further evaluation: history, physical exam, diagnostic testing, genetic testing, biopsy
- Medical management: RAAS inhibition (ACEi/ARB) x 4-6 weeks, then add immunosuppression
- If nonresponsive → revisit genetic testing; discontinue ineffective IS; continue RAAS blockade (IPNA Strong Rec Level X)
- Nonhereditary disease → consider clinical trial enrollment
- ESKD secondary to SRNS → kidney transplant (IPNA Moderate Rec Level B)

### Volume and Edema Management

**SSNS (IPNA 2022):**
- Evaluate volume status (IPNA Strong Rec Level A)
- Routine fluid restriction NOT recommended (IPNA Moderate Rec Level C)
- Low-salt diet for moderate-severe edema (max 2-3 mEq/kg/day)
- ACEi/ARBs NOT recommended during relapse for edema control (IPNA Strong Rec Level X)
- **Hypovolemia signs (tachycardia, cap refill, oliguria, AKI):** withhold diuretics (IPNA Strong Rec Level X), discontinue ACEi/ARBs (IPNA Strong Rec Level X)
- **Severe edema:** albumin 20-25% 0.5-1 g/kg IV over 4-6 hours + furosemide 1-2 mg/kg IV mid/end of infusion

### Thrombosis Prevention

- During acute episode: avoid immobilization (IPNA Strong Rec Level X), avoid intravascular volume contraction (IPNA Moderate Rec Level C)
- During relapse: support regular physical activity (IPNA Strong Rec Level A)
- Routine prophylactic anticoagulation NOT recommended for SSNS (IPNA Weak Rec Level C)
- Consider prophylactic anticoagulation if increased risk: prior VTE, central line, familial thrombophilia

### Infection Prevention

- Antibiotic prophylaxis NOT routinely given (IPNA Weak Rec Level C)
- If suspected bacterial infection → treat (IPNA Strong Rec Level A)
- Peritonitis → IV antibiotics targeting encapsulated organisms (IPNA Strong Rec Level A)
- Vaccinations: pneumococcal, meningococcal, varicella, annual influenza (IPNA Strong Rec Level A)
- Live vaccines contraindicated during immunosuppressive therapy or prednisone >= 20 mg/day; defer until off for 1-3 months
- Varicella exposure: VZV immunoglobulin (VZIG) if no prior immunity (IPNA Strong Rec Level A); or acyclovir 10 mg/kg PO QID x 7d

## Nephronophthisis (NPHP)

### Key Facts

- **Autosomal recessive** renal ciliopathy → cystic kidney disease + fibrosis → ESKD in first 3 decades
- **Most common genetic cause of ESKD in children** (exam classic)
- Incidence: 1/50,000 to 1/1,000,000 live births
- Males = females equally affected
- At least 25 causative genes identified, all affecting primary cilia formation/function

### Subtypes by Age of ESKD

| Subtype | ESKD onset | Key features |
|---|---|---|
| Infantile | < 3 years (often within 1st year) | May present in utero (oligohydramnios, enlarged cystic kidneys); situs inversus; cardiac malformations |
| **Juvenile** (most common) | Median 13 years | Growth impairment, polydipsia, polyuria, anemia resistant to iron |
| Adolescent/adult | Median 19 years | Similar to juvenile but later progression |

### Clinical Presentation

**Renal features:**
- Polyuria, polydipsia (concentrating defect — salt wasting)
- Growth impairment/failure to thrive
- Anemia (resistant to iron therapy — characteristic)
- Bland urine sediment (no hematuria, little/no proteinuria early)
- **Hypertension usually ABSENT** (unlike most CKD — due to salt wasting)
- Normal or small kidneys with corticomedullary cysts on US (NOT enlarged like ADPKD)

**Exam pearl:** Child with CKD + polyuria + no HTN + bland urine + normal-to-small kidneys with corticomedullary cysts → NPHP until proven otherwise. Contrast with ADPKD (enlarged kidneys, HTN, hematuria).

### Extrarenal Manifestations (Syndromic NPHP)

80-90% have isolated NPHP (no extrarenal findings). Syndromic forms include:

| Syndrome | Associations | Key gene |
|---|---|---|
| Senior-Loken | Retinitis pigmentosa + NPHP | Multiple NPHP genes |
| Joubert | Cerebellar vermis hypoplasia ("molar tooth sign") + NPHP | Multiple |
| Bardet-Biedl | Obesity + retinitis pigmentosa + polydactyly + hypogonadism + NPHP | BBS genes |
| COACH | Cerebellar vermis hypoplasia + oligophrenia + ataxia + coloboma + hepatic fibrosis | Multiple |
| Jeune (asphyxiating thoracic dystrophy) | Skeletal dysplasia + NPHP | Multiple |
| Meckel-Gruber | Posterior encephalocele + polydactyly + hepatic fibrosis + NPHP (lethal) | Multiple |

### Diagnosis

- Clinical: CKD + characteristic US (normal/small kidneys, ↑ echogenicity, corticomedullary cysts, loss of corticomedullary differentiation)
- **Genetic testing:** confirms diagnosis in 30-40% of cases
- Biopsy (if needed): tubulointerstitial nephritis, tubular atrophy, fibrosis, cyst formation at corticomedullary junction

### Management

- Slow CKD progression (standard CKD management)
- **Kidney transplant preferred over dialysis** — disease does NOT recur in transplanted kidney (exam pearl)
- Growth hormone for CKD-related height deficits
- Screen for extrarenal manifestations (ophthalmologic exams, hepatic US, skeletal imaging if syndromic)

## Renal Tubular Acidosis (RTA) in Children

### Overview

RTA = hyperchloremic, **normal AG** metabolic acidosis caused by renal tubular dysfunction → H+ retention or HCO3- wasting.

**Exam pearl:** In children, RTA is usually primary (genetic). In adults, RTA is usually secondary (drugs, autoimmune).

### Classification

| Type | Defect | Urine pH | Serum K+ | Classic pediatric associations |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1 (distal)** | Cannot secrete H+ in collecting duct | > 5.5 (cannot acidify) | Low/normal | Hereditary (most common in children); nephrocalcinosis; growth failure |
| **2 (proximal)** | Cannot reabsorb HCO3- in PCT | < 5.5 (after threshold exceeded) | Low | Fanconi syndrome, cystinosis, Lowe syndrome, Dent disease |
| **3 (combined)** | CA2 deficiency → both defects | Variable | Variable | Osteopetrosis, cerebral calcification, intellectual disability |
| **4 (hyperkalemic)** | Aldosterone deficiency/resistance | < 5.5 | **HIGH** | Congenital adrenal hyperplasia, PHA, obstructive uropathy |

### Type 1 (Distal) RTA — Pediatric Focus

**Hereditary causes (primary in children):**
- AD or AR inheritance
- AR pathogenic variants in H+-ATPase subunit genes (common in neonates)
- AD/AR variants in chloride/bicarbonate exchanger (AE1) gene
- AR forms: typically present in early childhood with failure to thrive, growth impairment
- Hearing loss can occur with certain H+-ATPase variants → screen for hearing loss

**Secondary causes:** Sjogren (rare in neonatal period), SLE, amphotericin B, lithium, ifosfamide, aminoglycosides, calcineurin inhibitors

**Exam pearl:** Child with failure to thrive + NAGMA + urine pH > 5.5 + nephrocalcinosis → distal RTA. If sensorineural hearing loss present → AR H+-ATPase mutation.

### Type 2 (Proximal) RTA — Pediatric Focus

Most common causes in children:
1. **Cystinosis** (most common cause of Fanconi syndrome in children)
2. Idiopathic Fanconi syndrome
3. Medication toxicity (ifosfamide, aminoglycosides)

Associated with Fanconi syndrome features: glycosuria, phosphaturia, aminoaciduria, uricosuria, low-molecular-weight proteinuria

**Exam pearl:** Topiramate is a common cause of type 2 RTA as a carbonic anhydrase inhibitor (Clinicians' Practice Point from DynaMed).

### Type 3 (Combined) RTA

- Rare; CA2 gene mutation → combined proximal and distal defects
- Triad: osteopetrosis + cerebral calcification + RTA
- Facial dysmorphism (hypertelorism, low-set ears, depressed nasal bridge)
- Hearing loss, cognitive impairment

### Type 4 (Hyperkalemic) RTA

Primary causes in children:
- Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (21-hydroxylase deficiency → mineralocorticoid deficiency)
- Aldosterone synthase deficiency
- Pseudohypoaldosteronism type 1: autosomal dominant (milder) or recessive (severe — pulmonary infections, myalgias, paralysis)
- PHA type 2 (Gordon syndrome): low-dose thiazide diuretics
- PHA type 3: fluids and antibiotics

**Neonatal consideration:** Physiologic aldosterone resistance in neonates → transient hyperkalemia after birth is common, especially in preterm infants.

### Management by Type

| Type | Alkali dose | Key considerations |
|---|---|---|
| Type 1 (distal) | NaHCO3 2-3 mEq/kg/day (infants need more: 4-8 mmol/kg/day) | Supplement K+ if persistent hypokalemia; diet counseling for nephrolithiasis prevention |
| Type 2 (proximal) | Higher doses: 10-15 mEq/kg/day (range 5-30) | Use K+-containing preparations (K citrate) to prevent worsening hypokalemia; consider thiazides to reduce HCO3- delivery |
| Type 3 (combined) | Alkali to halt/reverse growth deficiency | Primarily case-report-based protocols |
| Type 4 (hyperkalemic) | Alkali 2-3 mEq/kg/day | Treat underlying cause; dietary K+ restriction; loop diuretics; low-dose fludrocortisone if mineralocorticoid deficient |

**Treatment goals:** Normal blood pH and HCO3- levels (> 20 mEq/L in infants, > 22 mEq/L in older children). Normalize growth and bone minerite.

## Multicystic Dysplastic Kidney (MCDK)

### Key Facts

- Renal parenchyma replaced by multiple noncommunicating cysts of varying sizes + dysplastic tissue
- Most common form of cystic kidney in children
- Incidence: 1/2,400 to 1/4,300 live births
- Male:female = 2.4:1; females twice as likely bilateral
- Usually unilateral and detected on **prenatal ultrasound** (80-100% sensitivity)
- Bilateral disease = lethal

### Pathogenesis

- Obstruction of ureter at early embryogenesis → failed coordination of metanephros and branching ureteric bud
- Improper ureteric canalization at 8 weeks gestation

### Associated Conditions

- Contralateral VUR: 15-28% (most grade I-III)
- Contralateral kidney anomalies: 34% (VUR, UPJ obstruction, ureterocele, duplex kidney)
- Hypertension: associated but uncommon with treatment
- Wilms tumor: risk < 1/2,000; no increased risk with involuted kidney

### Diagnosis

**US features (characteristic):**
- Multiple thin-walled cysts that do NOT connect, distributed randomly
- Enlarged organ with irregular non-reniform outline
- No identifiable renal pelvis or ureter
- Hyperechogenic tissue between cysts
- No renal arterial flow on Doppler

**DDx:** hydronephrotic kidney (communicating cysts, identifiable pelvis), Wilms tumor, RCC with cystic components

### Management and Prognosis

- **Conservative management** in most cases (non-surgical)
- Most undergo spontaneous involution: complete involution in 33% at 2 years, 47% at 5 years, 59% at 10 years
- Follow-up US: every 3-6 months first year → q6 months second year → annually after
- Laparoscopic nephrectomy only if symptomatic (pain, poor feeding, increasing abdominal distension, HTN)
- Long-term: monitor BP, kidney function, contralateral kidney
- **29% had kidney damage** (composite of reduced GFR, HTN, proteinuria) at mean 10.3 years follow-up (Pediatr Nephrol 2024)

## Dehydration and Hypovolemia in Children

### Definitions

- **Dehydration:** excess water loss > sodium loss → intracellular volume loss
- **Hypovolemia:** loss of extracellular total body water and salts → intravascular depletion
- TBW: 75% of body weight in full-term neonate; 50-60% in adolescent

### Severity Classification (CDC)

| Severity | Weight loss | Mental status | Mucous membranes | Skin turgor | Urine output |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Minimal/none | < 3% | Alert | Moist | Normal (instant recoil) | Normal/decreased |
| Mild-moderate | 3-9% | Restless/irritable | Dry, eyes slightly sunken | Decreased (< 2s recoil) | Decreased |
| Severe | > 9% | Lethargic/unconscious | Parched, eyes deeply sunken | Markedly decreased (> 2s) | Minimal |

### Types by Sodium

| Type | Plasma Na+ | Mechanism | Fluid shift |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hypernatremic (hypertonic) | > 145 | Greater water loss than Na loss | ECF → ICF (cells swell → risk of cerebral edema with rapid correction) |
| Isotonic | 135-145 | Proportional loss | No shift |
| Hyponatremic (hypotonic) | < 135 | Na-rich losses replaced with hypotonic fluids | ECF → ICF |

### Management

- **Mild-moderate:** ORS 50-100 mL/kg over 4 hours (Strong Recommendation)
- Consider nasogastric if child cannot drink but normal mental status (Conditional Rec)
- **Severe/shock:** rapid IV with LR or NS 10-20 mL/kg boluses until shock resolves (Strong Rec)
- Then calculate deficit + maintenance; replace deficit over 24-48 hours
- **Hypernatremic dehydration:** correct Na slowly (< 10 mEq/L per 24 hours) to avoid cerebral edema

**Exam pearl:** In children with gastroenteritis-related dehydration without bloody diarrhea, lab tests are often unnecessary. Clinical dehydration scale (appearance + eyes + mucous membranes + tears) guides management.

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

1. **6-year-old with periorbital edema, UPCR 3.5 g/g, albumin 1.8 g/dL:** Classic childhood NS. Age 1-12, no hematuria/HTN/low C3 → assume MCD. Start prednisone 60 mg/m2/day. No biopsy needed.

2. **Child with CKD, polyuria, no HTN, normal-sized kidneys with corticomedullary cysts, bland urine:** Nephronophthisis. Order genetic testing. Disease will NOT recur after transplant.

3. **Infant with failure to thrive, NAGMA, urine pH 6.8, nephrocalcinosis:** Distal RTA (type 1). Start NaHCO3 4-8 mmol/kg/day. Screen for hearing loss.

4. **Neonate with sCr 1.2 mg/dL on day 1 of life:** This may reflect MATERNAL creatinine (placental transfer). Recheck at 48-72 hours. Neonatal sCr should decline to 0.2-0.4 mg/dL by 1-2 weeks.

5. **Child with steroid-resistant NS after 4 weeks of prednisone:** Biopsy (likely FSGS). Genetic testing. Start RAAS blockade x 4-6 weeks. If no response to IS → consider trial enrollment; if ESKD → transplant.

6. **Prenatal US shows unilateral multicystic kidney:** MCDK. Postnatal US to confirm. Check contralateral kidney for VUR (15-28%). Conservative management. Most involute spontaneously.

7. **5-year-old with nephrotic syndrome relapsing 5 times in 12 months:** Frequently relapsing SSNS. Low-dose prednisone maintenance (IPNA Strong Rec Level A). If uncontrolled → corticosteroid-sparing agent (MMF or CNI or rituximab).
