# Potassium Disorders — Clinical Approach

## Exam Logic Summary

Potassium questions are among the highest-yield electrolyte topics. The exam tests: (1) classification of hyperkalemia severity and ECG changes, (2) stepwise emergency management (membrane stabilization → shift → removal), (3) systematic differential of hypokalemia using urine K⁺ and acid-base status, (4) distinguishing transcellular shift from true K⁺ deficit, (5) medication-induced dyskalemia. Common distractors: confusing pseudohyperkalemia with true hyperkalemia; forgetting to correct magnesium in refractory hypokalemia; choosing SPS over newer binders.

**Textbook ref:** Brenner Ch. 15 (Potassium Homeostasis), Harrison Ch. 53 (Electrolyte Disorders), Daugirdas Ch. 10 (Potassium in Dialysis)

---

## Normal Physiology

- Total body K⁺ ~3,500 mEq; 98% intracellular (maintained by Na⁺-K⁺-ATPase)
- Normal serum K⁺: 3.5-5.0 mEq/L
- Daily intake ~40-120 mEq; 90% excreted by kidney (principal cells, collecting duct), 10% GI
- Key regulators: aldosterone (↑ K⁺ secretion), insulin (↑ cellular uptake), catecholamines (beta-2 → cellular uptake)

# HYPERKALEMIA

## Definition and Classification

| Severity | Serum K⁺ (mEq/L) | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Mild | 5.0-5.5 | Monitor, investigate cause |
| Moderate | 5.5-6.5 | Investigate, consider treatment |
| Severe | > 6.5 | Emergency — ECG + immediate treatment |

**Exam pearl:** Serum K⁺ > 6.0 mEq/L may cause neuromuscular dysfunction. Acute management is REQUIRED for K⁺ > 6.5 mEq/L regardless of symptoms, OR > 5.5 mEq/L with ECG changes or symptoms.

## Pseudohyperkalemia

Must be ruled out FIRST by repeat measurement [Strong Recommendation].

| Cause | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Hemolyzed sample | K⁺ released from RBCs during collection |
| Fist clenching during draw | Local K⁺ release from muscle |
| Tourniquet too long | Ischemia → K⁺ release |
| Leukocytosis (> 100K) | K⁺ release from fragile WBCs in vitro |
| Thrombocytosis (> 500K) | K⁺ release from platelets during clotting |

**Exam pearl:** Paired lithium heparin (plasma) + clotted serum samples differentiate pseudohyperkalemia. Plasma K⁺ lower than serum K⁺ by > 0.4 mEq/L suggests pseudohyperkalemia. Whole blood potassium (POC blood gas) can also help.

## Etiology — Systematic Approach

### If Kidney Function Impaired → Decreased Excretion

- CKD stage 4-5 / AKI (most common cause in hospitalized patients)
- Medications impairing renal K⁺ excretion: ACEi, ARBs, K⁺-sparing diuretics (spironolactone, amiloride, triamterene), trimethoprim, calcineurin inhibitors, NSAIDs, heparin
- Hypoaldosteronism (Type IV RTA, Addison disease)
- High dietary K⁺ intake in setting of impaired excretion

### If Kidney Function Normal → Transcellular Shift or Other

| Mechanism | Causes |
|---|---|
| Transcellular shift (ICF → ECF) | Acidosis (metabolic > respiratory), insulin deficiency/DKA, beta-blockers, digoxin toxicity, succinylcholine, arginine HCl, hypertonicity, rhabdomyolysis/tumor lysis |
| Hypoaldosteronism | Adrenal insufficiency, hyporenin-hypoaldosteronism (Type IV RTA), drugs (ACEi, ARB, heparin, ketoconazole) |
| Increased intake (rare alone) | K⁺ supplements, salt substitutes, blood transfusions (stored blood) |

**Exam pearl:** Normal kidney function + hyperkalemia → think transcellular shift FIRST (acidosis, insulin deficiency, beta-blockers). Isolated increased intake rarely causes hyperkalemia with normal kidneys.

## ECG Changes (MUST MEMORIZE — Progressive Sequence)

| Stage | K⁺ Level (approx) | ECG Finding |
|---|---|---|
| Early | 5.5-6.5 | **Peaked (tall, narrow) T waves** |
| Moderate | 6.5-7.0 | PR prolongation, P wave flattening |
| Advanced | 7.0-8.0 | QRS widening |
| Severe | > 8.0 | Sine wave pattern → VF → asystole |

**Exam pearl:** ECG changes do NOT reliably correlate with exact K⁺ level. Some patients have VF at 6.5, others tolerate 8.0. ECG is URGENT at K⁺ >= 6.0 mEq/L (UKKA Grade 1B). Continuous 3-lead monitoring if K⁺ 6.0-6.4 with high risk or rapid rise, or K⁺ >= 6.5.

## Workup When K⁺ >= 5.5 mEq/L

- BUN, creatinine (kidney function)
- Blood gas (acidosis)
- CPK (rhabdomyolysis)
- Drug levels (digoxin, tacrolimus)
- LDH (hemolysis)
- Uric acid, phosphorus (tumor lysis)
- Cortisol, renin, aldosterone (hypoaldosteronism)
- CBC (leukocytosis/thrombocytosis for pseudohyperK)
- Calcium (hypocalcemia exacerbates cardiac effects)

## Acute Management — Stepwise Protocol

### Step 1: Cardiac Membrane Stabilization

| Agent | Dose | Onset | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Calcium chloride 10%** | 10 mL IV over 5 min | 1-3 min | **Preferred in cardiac arrest/peri-arrest** [Strong Rec] |
| **Calcium gluconate 10%** | 30 mL IV over 10 min | 1-3 min | Preferred if NOT in arrest (less tissue necrosis risk); NOT preferred in arrest |

**Exam pearl:** Calcium does NOT lower K⁺. It stabilizes myocardial membrane. If ECG persists abnormal after 5 min → repeat dose. CaCl has 3x more elemental Ca²⁺ than Ca-gluconate per volume. Equivalent elemental dose: 13.6 mEq (6.8 mmol).

### Step 2: Transcellular Shift (K⁺ Into Cells)

| Agent | Dose | Onset | Duration | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Insulin-glucose** | 10 units regular insulin in 25g glucose IV | 15-30 min | 4-6 h | First-line for shift [Strong Rec for severe; Conditional for moderate]. Monitor glucose — check if < 126 mg/dL before glucose infusion |
| **Nebulized albuterol** | 10-20 mg nebulized | 15-30 min | 2-4 h | Adjunct (not sole therapy). [Strong Rec severe, Conditional moderate]. Additive with insulin |
| **NaHCO3** | Consider if acidemic | 30-60 min | Variable | Conditional Rec — avoid routine use. May help in acidosis. Can be adjunctive in cardiac arrest |

**Exam pearl:** Insulin-glucose is the most reliable shift agent. Classic exam pitfall: forgetting to give glucose with insulin → hypoglycemia. Follow with glucose infusion (10% dextrose 50 mL/h for 5 hours) if glucose < 126 mg/dL (7 mmol/L) at time of treatment.

### Step 3: Total Body K⁺ Removal

| Agent | Route | Onset | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Loop diuretics** | IV NS + furosemide | 30-60 min | If nonoliguric and volume replete |
| **Sodium zirconium cyclosilicate (SZC)** | Oral | 1-4 h | Preferred potassium binder. [Strong Rec] severe (>= 6.5) or moderate (6.0-6.4). Order of preference: SZC > patiromer > SPS |
| **Patiromer** | Oral | 4-7 h | [Conditional Rec] for emergency; [Strong Rec] for RAAS therapy maintenance. Slower onset than SZC |
| **Dialysis** | HD or CRRT | Immediate | If refractory to above, or oliguric/anuric kidney failure |

**Exam pearl:** Avoid SPS (sodium polystyrene sulfonate / Kayexalate) and CPS (calcium polystyrene sulfonate) for acute hyperkalemia [Strong Recommendation]. Risk of intestinal necrosis. SZC and patiromer are preferred newer agents.

### Step 4: Treat Underlying Cause

- Review and adjust K⁺-increasing medications
- Correct acidosis
- Treat AKI

## Chronic Management

- Treat non-dietary causes first (constipation, acidosis, poorly controlled diabetes)
- Dietary K⁺ restriction if CKD + persistent hyperK > 5.5 mEq/L
- NaHCO3 if serum HCO3⁻ < 22 mmol/L [Strong Rec]
- Loop diuretics (adjunctive if nonoliguric, volume replete)
- Potassium binders for persistent hyperK (>= 6.0) in CKD stage 3b-5 (not on dialysis) or heart failure:
  - SZC if suboptimal RAASi dose or RAASi stopped [Strong Rec]
  - Patiromer if suboptimal RAASi or not on RAASi [Strong Rec]
- Short-term SPS for patients not meeting SZC/patiromer criteria [Conditional Rec]

## RAASi Management in Hyperkalemia

- **Mild hyperK (5.5-5.9):** Increase monitoring, consider dose reduction of RAASi [Strong Rec]
- **Discontinue RAASi during acute illness** (sepsis, hypovolemia, AKI) regardless of K⁺ [Strong Rec]
- **Stop RAASi if K⁺ >= 6.0** and patient does not meet criteria for SZC/patiromer [Strong Rec]
- Goal: maintain RAASi therapy using K⁺ binders when possible (cardiorenal benefit)

# HYPOKALEMIA

## Definition and Classification

| Severity | Serum K⁺ (mEq/L) | Features |
|---|---|---|
| Mild | 3.0-3.5 | Often asymptomatic |
| Moderate | 2.5-3.0 | Muscle weakness, cramps, fatigue |
| Severe | < 2.5 | Paralysis, rhabdomyolysis, arrhythmias, respiratory failure |

**Exam pearl:** Symptoms usually occur at K⁺ < 2.5 mEq/L — muscle pain, cramps, weakness, fatigue, constipation, syncope, palpitations.

## Pseudohypokalemia

- Severe leukocytosis (AML, > 100K WBCs) → WBCs take up K⁺ in vitro if sample sits at room temperature
- Prevent by rapid processing or refrigeration of sample

## ECG Changes (Progressive)

| Stage | Finding |
|---|---|
| Early (K⁺ 3.0-3.5) | T wave flattening, ST depression |
| Moderate (K⁺ 2.5-3.0) | Prominent U waves |
| Severe (K⁺ < 2.5) | T-U fusion, QT prolongation, VT/VF risk |

**Exam pearl:** U wave = classic hypokalemia sign. Hypokalemia enhances digoxin toxicity — always check K⁺ in patients on digoxin.

## Etiology — Diagnostic Algorithm

### Step 1: Assess Potassium Intake

- Low dietary intake alone rarely causes hypokalemia (kidney conserves well)
- But malnutrition, anorexia, alcoholism can contribute

### Step 2: Urine K⁺:Cr Ratio (Spot Urine)

| Urine K⁺:Cr | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| < 13 mEq/g (< 1.5 mEq/mmol) | **Extrarenal loss** (GI) or transcellular shift |
| > 13 mEq/g (> 1.5 mEq/mmol) | **Renal potassium wasting** |

### Step 3: If Renal Wasting → Check Blood Pressure

**Hypokalemia + Hypertension:**

| Aldosterone | Renin | Cortisol | Diagnosis |
|---|---|---|---|
| High | High | Normal | Renal artery stenosis, reninoma, malignant HTN |
| High | Low | Normal | **Primary aldosteronism** (Conn syndrome) |
| Low | Low | Low-normal | **Liddle syndrome**, licorice ingestion |
| Low | Low | High | **Cushing syndrome** |

**Hypokalemia + Normal/Low BP:**

Check acid-base status:
- **Metabolic acidosis:** diarrhea, laxative abuse, DKA correction, RTA (type I or II)
- **Metabolic alkalosis:** vomiting, diuretics, **Bartter syndrome**, **Gitelman syndrome**, Liddle syndrome

### Step 4: If Metabolic Alkalosis → Check Urine Chloride

| Urine Cl⁻ | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| < 25 mEq/L | Cl⁻-responsive: vomiting, post-diuretic |
| > 25 mEq/L | Cl⁻-resistant: active diuretic use, Bartter, Gitelman |

### Step 5: If Cl⁻-Resistant → Check Urine Ca²⁺:Cr Ratio

| Urine Ca/Cr ratio | Diagnosis |
|---|---|
| > 0.26 mg/mg | **Bartter syndrome** or loop diuretic use |
| < 0.02 mg/mg | **Gitelman syndrome** or thiazide diuretic use |

**Exam pearl:** This is the CRITICAL differentiator: Bartter = hypercalciuria (like furosemide blocks NKCC2 in TAL → impairs paracellular Ca²⁺ reabsorption). Gitelman = hypocalciuria (like thiazide blocks NCC in DCT → enhanced proximal Ca²⁺ reabsorption).

## Potassium Replacement

### Estimation

- **10 mEq K⁺ supplementation raises serum K⁺ by ~0.1 mEq/L** (rough guide)
- K⁺ deficit for each 1 mEq/L drop below 4.0: ~200-400 mEq total body deficit

### Oral Replacement

- Preferred for mild-moderate hypokalemia
- Max 40-100 mEq/day in divided doses
- KCl preferred (replaces Cl⁻ deficit too — critical for Cl⁻-responsive alkalosis)

### IV Replacement

| Indication | K⁺ < 2.5 mEq/L, unable to take PO, arrhythmias, digoxin toxicity |
|---|---|
| Standard | KCl 40 mEq in 1L NS, rate 10-20 mEq/h |
| Max rate | 40 mEq/h (peripheral); requires continuous cardiac monitoring if > 10 mEq/h |
| Do NOT use | Dextrose-containing fluids (insulin release → further K⁺ shift into cells) |

**Exam pearl:** ALWAYS correct magnesium FIRST in refractory hypokalemia. Hypomagnesemia causes renal K⁺ wasting via ROMK channel dysregulation. Hypokalemia will NOT correct until Mg²⁺ is repleted.

### Monitoring

- Severe (< 2.5): check K⁺ every 1-6 hours
- Moderate: daily
- Admit with cardiac monitoring if: ECG changes, K⁺ < 2.5, IV rate > 10 mEq/h

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

1. **Post-cardiac arrest + wide QRS:** Think hyperK → immediate IV calcium chloride (not gluconate — arrest setting) → insulin-glucose → consider emergent dialysis. ECG interpretation is key.

2. **CKD patient on ACEi + spironolactone with K⁺ 6.2:** Moderate hyperK on dual RAASi + K⁺-sparing diuretic. Management: hold spironolactone, consider RAASi dose reduction, start SZC or patiromer to enable RAASi continuation.

3. **Young woman with chronic hypokalemia, metabolic alkalosis, normal BP, low urine calcium:** Classic Gitelman syndrome. Confirm with genetic testing (SLC12A3 mutation). Treatment: lifelong oral Mg²⁺ + KCl supplementation.

4. **Insulin drip patient with declining K⁺:** Insulin shifts K⁺ intracellularly. Must add K⁺ to IV fluids when treating DKA. Check K⁺ every 2 hours during insulin infusion.

5. **Hypokalemia refractory to KCl replacement:** Check Mg²⁺ level. If low, correct Mg²⁺ first. If Mg²⁺ normal, consider ongoing renal losses (diuretics, Bartter/Gitelman, RTA).

6. **Patient on digoxin with K⁺ 3.0:** Hypokalemia potentiates digoxin toxicity. Replace K⁺ even if patient is asymptomatic. Target K⁺ >= 4.0 in patients on digoxin.

7. **Hyperkalemia in dialysis patient:** SZC approved for use in dialysis. Avoid SPS. Consider dietary counseling, optimize dialysis adequacy, and use low-K⁺ dialysate (but avoid dialysate K⁺ < 2 mEq/L — arrhythmia risk).
