# Sodium and Water Disorders — Clinical Approach

## Exam Logic Summary

Sodium disorders are fundamentally WATER disorders, not sodium disorders. The exam tests: (1) systematic classification of hyponatremia by osmolality then volume status, (2) correction rate limits and osmotic demyelination risk, (3) SIAD diagnostic criteria vs. other causes of euvolemic hyponatremia, (4) water deprivation test interpretation for DI, (5) central vs. nephrogenic DI differentiation. Common distractors: treating hypovolemic hyponatremia with fluid restriction (wrong — needs NS); confusing SIAD with cerebral salt wasting; overcorrecting chronic hyponatremia.

**Textbook ref:** Brenner Ch. 14 (Sodium and Water Balance), Harrison Ch. 52 (Water and Sodium Disorders), Daugirdas Ch. 11 (Sodium in Dialysis)

---

## Core Concepts

- Serum Na⁺ reflects water balance (tonicity), NOT total body sodium
- Serum Na⁺ = total body solute / total body water
- Hyponatremia = relative water excess; Hypernatremia = relative water deficit
- ADH (arginine vasopressin, AVP) is the master regulator of free water excretion
- Osmolality formula: 2 x Na⁺ + glucose/18 + BUN/2.8 (all in mg/dL)

# HYPONATREMIA

## Definition

Serum Na⁺ < 135 mEq/L. Most common electrolyte disorder.

| Severity | Na⁺ (mEq/L) | Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| Mild | 130-135 | Often asymptomatic |
| Moderate | 125-129 | Nausea, headache, confusion |
| Severe | < 125 | Seizures, obtundation, respiratory failure, coma, death |

## Classification by Duration

| Type | Duration | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Acute** | < 48 hours | Brain edema → herniation risk. Aggressive correction safe |
| **Chronic** | >= 48 hours (or unknown) | Brain adapted. **Osmotic demyelination syndrome (ODS) risk if corrected too fast** |

**Exam pearl:** If duration unknown → treat as CHRONIC (safer). Chronic is more common than acute.

## Systematic Diagnostic Approach

### Step 1: Serum Osmolality

| Serum Osm | Type | Causes |
|---|---|---|
| < 270-280 | **Hypotonic** (true hyponatremia) | Most causes → proceed to Step 2 |
| 280-295 | **Isotonic** (pseudohyponatremia) | Hyperlipidemia, hyperproteinemia (lab artifact with indirect ISE) |
| > 295 | **Hypertonic** (translocational) | Hyperglycemia (glucose draws water), mannitol, glycine |

**Exam pearl:** For hyperglycemia: corrected Na⁺ = measured Na⁺ + 1.6 mEq/L per 100 mg/dL glucose above 100. Some use 2.4 for glucose > 400.

### Step 2: Volume Status (for Hypotonic Hyponatremia)

| Volume Status | Urine Na⁺ | Causes |
|---|---|---|
| **Hypovolemic** | < 20 → extrarenal loss | Vomiting, diarrhea, burns, third-spacing |
| **Hypovolemic** | > 20 → renal loss | Diuretics (thiazides!), adrenal insufficiency, cerebral salt wasting, osmotic diuresis |
| **Euvolemic** | > 30 (usually) | **SIAD** (most common), hypothyroidism, adrenal insufficiency, drugs, reset osmostat |
| **Hypervolemic** | < 20 (usually) | CHF, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome |
| **Hypervolemic** | > 20 | Advanced CKD/ESKD |

**Exam pearl:** Thiazide diuretics cause hyponatremia (impair diluting capacity in DCT). Loop diuretics rarely cause hyponatremia (impair concentrating AND diluting ability). This is a classic exam question.

### Step 3: Urine Osmolality

| Urine Osm | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| < 100 mOsm/kg | Suppressed ADH: primary polydipsia, beer potomania, low-solute diet (tea-and-toast) |
| > 100 mOsm/kg | Impaired water excretion: SIAD, hypovolemia, edematous states, cortisol/thyroid deficiency |

### Step 4: FENa and FEUrate

- FENa may not be accurate if patient on diuretics → use **FEUrate** (Fractional Excretion of Urate)
- Urine Osm > serum Osm + urine Na⁺ > 30-40 mEq/L after volume correction → likely SIAD

## Management of Hyponatremia

### Severe Symptoms (seizures, obtundation, respiratory failure)

Regardless of Na⁺ level or chronicity:
1. **3% NaCl bolus:** 100-150 mL IV over 10-20 min [Strong Rec]
2. May repeat up to 3 times (expected increase 2-3 mEq/L per 100 mL bolus)
3. Goal: increase Na⁺ by 4-6 mEq/L in first few hours to arrest symptoms
4. Once symptoms improve → stop hypertonic saline, switch to diagnosis-based treatment

### Moderate Symptoms (Na⁺ 125-129, nausea/headache)

1. Stop offending medications [Strong Rec]
2. 3% NaCl: 150 mL IV over 20 min OR 0.5-1 mL/kg/h infusion [Conditional Rec]
3. Target: symptom improvement

### Correction Rate Limits (CRITICAL)

| Risk Category | 24-hour Goal | 48-hour Goal |
|---|---|---|
| Low risk of ODS | 8-12 mEq/L/day | - |
| **High risk of ODS** (malnutrition, alcoholism, severe hyponatremia, K⁺ < 3.0) | **6-8 mEq/L/day** | - |

**Exam pearl:** Maximum safe correction = 10-12 mEq/L in first 24 hours, then 6-8 mEq/L in any subsequent 24-hour period [Strong Rec]. ODS risk factors: chronic hyponatremia, Na⁺ < 120, hypokalemia, malnutrition, alcoholism, liver disease.

### Overcorrection Rescue

If Na⁺ increases > 10-12 in 24h or > 6-8 in any subsequent 24h:
1. **Stop all active treatments** [Strong Rec]
2. **Desmopressin (DDAVP) 2 mcg IV** q8h [Strong Rec]
3. **D5W infusion** (10 mL/kg over 1 hour) [Strong Rec]
4. Strict urine output and Na⁺ monitoring

**Exam pearl:** Proactive DDAVP strategy — some protocols give DDAVP preemptively with hypertonic saline to prevent aquaresis-induced overcorrection, especially in hypovolemic patients who will autocorrect once volume is restored.

### Treatment by Volume Status

| Hypovolemic | NS (0.9% NaCl) + treat cause. Watch for rapid autocorrection when volume restored |
|---|---|
| Euvolemic (SIAD) | **Fluid restriction <= 1 L/day** [Conditional Rec] first-line. Second-line: oral urea 0.25-0.5 g/kg/day, low-dose loop + NaCl tabs. Avoid demeclocycline/lithium [Strong Rec] |
| Hypervolemic | Fluid restriction + treat underlying disease (CHF/cirrhosis). Loop diuretics. Vaptans controversial |
| Hypokalemia coexists | **Correct K⁺ with KCl** — K⁺ correction contributes to Na⁺ correction (include in calculation!) |

### Vaptans (Arginine Vasopressin Receptor Antagonists)

- Tolvaptan (V2 selective, oral), conivaptan (V1a/V2, IV)
- May be considered for euvolemic or hypervolemic hyponatremia
- **NOT for hypovolemic hyponatremia** [Strong Rec against]
- Limited evidence for improved clinical outcomes
- Risk: overcorrection, hepatotoxicity (tolvaptan)
- Cost limits long-term use

# SYNDROME OF INAPPROPRIATE ANTIDIURESIS (SIAD)

## Definition

Euvolemic hypotonic hyponatremia due to nonphysiologic secretion of AVP (or V2 receptor activation), resulting in impaired free-water excretion.

Also called: SIADH (syndrome of inappropriate ADH secretion). Now renamed SIAD to include non-ADH mediated causes (nephrogenic SIAD from V2 receptor mutations).

## Diagnostic Criteria (ALL Must Be Met)

1. Serum Na⁺ < 135 mEq/L
2. **Plasma osmolality < 270 mOsm/kg**
3. **Urine osmolality > 100 mOsm/kg** (usually > 300)
4. **Urine Na⁺ > 30 mEq/L** (on normal salt + water intake)
5. Euvolemic (no edema, no dehydration)
6. **Exclusion of:** hypothyroidism, hypoadrenalism (cortisol deficiency), diuretic use, renal failure

**Exam pearl:** SIAD is a diagnosis of EXCLUSION. Must rule out adrenal insufficiency and hypothyroidism first. Hallmarks: subclinical volume expansion with low-to-normal creatinine, urea, uric acid, and potassium.

## SIAD Types (Copeptin-Based Classification)

| Type | Mechanism | Urine Osm | ADH Levels | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A (most common) | Unregulated ADH secretion | High, constant | High, random fluctuations | Endogenous (ectopic, CNS) |
| B | Neurohypophysis injury / exogenous ADH | High, constant | High | Exogenous or injury |
| C (rare) | Reset osmostat (lower set point) | Low | Inappropriately elevated for osm | Idiopathic |
| D (rare) | V2 receptor mutations (nephrogenic SIAD) | High | Undetectable | Nephrogenic |
| E (barostat) | Decreased nonosmotic inhibition | NA | Decreased | Endogenous |

## Common Causes (HIGH-YIELD)

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Malignancy** | Small cell lung cancer (#1 malignant cause), head/neck cancers, lymphoma |
| **CNS disorders** | Meningitis, encephalitis, stroke, SAH, TBI, brain tumor |
| **Pulmonary** | Pneumonia, TB, lung abscess, positive-pressure ventilation |
| **Drugs** | SSRIs (#1 drug cause), carbamazepine, cyclophosphamide, oxytocin, vincristine, opioids, ecstasy/MDMA |
| **Pain/nausea/stress** | Physiologic ADH release (SIAD-like state) — postoperative, exercise |
| **Idiopathic** | 10-20% of SIAD cases |

**Exam pearl:** Small cell lung cancer = ectopic ADH production. SSRIs = most common drug cause. Postoperative hyponatremia in young women = particularly dangerous (higher ODS risk with overcorrection).

## SIAD Management

| Setting | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Asymptomatic/mild | Fluid restriction <= 1 L/day [Conditional Rec] |
| Refractory | Oral urea 0.25-0.5 g/kg/day OR low-dose loop diuretic + oral NaCl |
| Symptomatic | 3% NaCl (see hyponatremia management above) |
| Severe/refractory | Vaptans (second-line for euvolemic) |
| Always | **Treat underlying cause** |

**Exam pearl:** Fluid restriction failure criteria: urine Osm > 500, urine Na⁺ + urine K⁺ > serum Na⁺ (electrolyte-free water clearance is negative → patient retaining water faster than restricting it).

# HYPERNATREMIA

## Definition

Serum Na⁺ > 145 mEq/L. Always indicates relative water deficit (hypertonic state).

| Severity | Na⁺ (mEq/L) |
|---|---|
| Mild | 146-150 |
| Moderate | 151-159 |
| Severe | >= 160 |

## Etiology — Always a Water Problem

| Mechanism | Causes |
|---|---|
| **Inadequate water intake** | Impaired thirst (elderly, infants, obtunded/intubated), no access to water |
| **Renal water loss** | Diabetes insipidus (central or nephrogenic), osmotic diuresis (glucose, urea, mannitol) |
| **Extrarenal water loss** | Insensible losses (fever, burns, ventilation), GI losses (diarrhea > vomiting) |
| **Sodium gain** (rare) | Hypertonic saline, NaHCO3, salt poisoning, primary hyperaldosteronism |

**Exam pearl:** Hypernatremia in an alert patient with water access = almost impossible (intact thirst prevents it). Think: hospitalized patient with restricted access, elderly with impaired thirst, infants.

## Workup

- Urine osmolality: most important test
  - Urine Osm > 800: intact ADH response → extrarenal loss or inadequate intake
  - Urine Osm 300-800: partial DI or osmotic diuresis
  - Urine Osm < 300 (< 200 in complete DI): **diabetes insipidus**
- Urine volume: > 3 L/day = polyuria → DI or osmotic diuresis
- Serum glucose, calcium, potassium (secondary causes of NDI)

## Water Deficit Formula

Water deficit (L) = TBW x [(serum Na⁺ / 140) - 1]

TBW = 0.6 x weight (men) or 0.5 x weight (women), adjusted for age/body composition.

## Correction Rate

| Setting | Rate |
|---|---|
| **Acute (< 48h)** | Can correct rapidly (1-2 mEq/L/h) |
| **Chronic (>= 48h or unknown)** | Max 10-12 mEq/L per 24 hours (risk of cerebral edema if corrected too fast) |

Replace with: D5W, 0.45% NaCl, or enteral free water. Account for ongoing losses.

# DIABETES INSIPIDUS

## Classification

| Type | Synonym | Defect | Urine Osm after DDAVP |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Central DI (AVP-D)** | Arginine vasopressin deficiency | Inadequate ADH production/release | **Urine Osm rises > 50%** (responds) |
| **Nephrogenic DI (AVP-R)** | Arginine vasopressin resistance | Kidney resistant to ADH | **Urine Osm does NOT rise** (< 50% increase) |

## AVP-D (Central Diabetes Insipidus)

### Definition
Congenital or acquired deficiency of AVP production/release from hypothalamic-pituitary axis. Kidneys cannot retain free water → polyuria, polydipsia, dilute urine.

### Clinical Features
- **Polyuria:** >= 3 L/day in adults, > 40-50 mL/kg/day
- **Polydipsia:** compensatory if thirst intact
- **Urine Osm:** typically < 800 mOsm/kg (often < 200 in complete)
- If thirst intact + water access → Na⁺ may be normal
- If adipsia/hypodipsia or restricted access → **hypernatremia**

### Epidemiology
- 1 in 25,000 individuals
- < 10% autoimmune/idiopathic
- Post-pituitary surgery: transient in up to 45%; permanent in 0-12.5%
- 20% of moderate-severe TBI; 15% of nontraumatic SAH

### Causes

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| Post-surgical | Transsphenoidal surgery (most common acquired cause), post-pituitary surgery |
| Neoplastic | Craniopharyngioma, germinoma, metastatic cancer, lymphoma, leukemia |
| Infiltrative/inflammatory | Langerhans cell histiocytosis, sarcoidosis, lymphocytic hypophysitis, GPA |
| Traumatic | TBI, neurosurgery |
| Vascular | Internal carotid aneurysm |
| Infectious | Meningitis, encephalitis, toxoplasmosis |
| Idiopathic/autoimmune | Anti-AVP antibodies; MRI may show pituitary stalk thickening |
| Genetic | Autosomal dominant (*AVP* gene mutations), Wolfram syndrome |

### Postoperative AVP-D Rates by Pathology

| Pathology | Any AVP-D (%) | Permanent (%) |
|---|---|---|
| Pituitary adenoma | 11.4 | 1.9 |
| Rathke cleft cyst | 26 | 10.3 |
| Craniopharyngioma | 40 | 26.3 |

**Exam pearl:** Triphasic response after pituitary surgery: (1) DI days 1-5 (axonal shock), (2) SIAD days 5-10 (uncontrolled AVP release from dying neurons), (3) permanent DI (if >= 80% of AVP neurons destroyed). Not all patients go through all 3 phases.

### Diagnosis
1. Confirm polyuria (> 3 L/day or > 40-50 mL/kg/day) with dilute urine (< 800 mOsm/kg)
2. If plasma Osm > 295 AND serum Na⁺ > 147 → DI confirmed, skip to DDAVP test
3. If equivocal → **water deprivation test** → then **DDAVP challenge**
4. Baseline **copeptin** > 21.4 pmol/L → AVP-R (complete or partial)
5. Hypertonic saline-stimulated copeptin < 4.9 pmol/L → AVP-D
6. MRI pituitary: look for stalk thickening, mass, absent posterior bright spot

### Treatment
- **Desmopressin (DDAVP)** = treatment of choice
  - Oral: start 0.05 mg at bedtime, titrate by 0.05 mg increments
  - Nasal spray: 10 mcg once daily, may increase to 40 mcg in 2-3 divided doses
  - IV/SC: 1-2 mcg (postoperative, adipsia, unable to take PO/nasal)
- If thirst intact → patients can self-regulate with DDAVP + ad lib drinking
- **Do NOT drink more than needed to satisfy thirst** when on DDAVP (hyponatremia risk)
- Monitor Na⁺ frequently in first 48 hours of dose adjustment
- Transient post-surgical AVP-D → give DDAVP on demand first few days (may resolve)
- **Associated conditions:** check for anterior pituitary deficiencies (GH most common), especially if idiopathic

## AVP-R (Nephrogenic Diabetes Insipidus)

### Definition
Kidney resistance to AVP action despite adequate or elevated AVP levels. Clinical triad: polyuria, polydipsia, hypernatremia.

### Key Numbers
- May produce up to 12 L/day of dilute urine
- Urine Osm < 300 mOsm/kg (often < 100 in complete)
- Does NOT respond to DDAVP administration (< 50% rise in urine Osm)

### Causes

**Acquired (> 90% of NDI in adults):**

| Cause | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Lithium** | #1 drug cause; 20-87% of patients on long-term lithium; may be irreversible |
| Hypercalcemia | Impairs AQP2 trafficking |
| Hypokalemia | Impairs medullary concentrating gradient |
| Medications | Foscarnet, aminoglycosides, amphotericin B, cisplatin, ifosfamide, demeclocycline, methoxyflurane, colchicine |
| Systemic disease | Sarcoidosis, amyloidosis, multiple myeloma, Sjogren, sickle cell |
| Post-obstructive | After relief of bilateral ureteral obstruction |
| Renal parenchymal | CKD (reduced medullary gradient), tubulointerstitial disease |

**Congenital (< 10%):**

| Gene | Protein | Inheritance | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| *AVPR2* | V2 vasopressin receptor | X-linked recessive | 90% of congenital NDI |
| *AQP2* | Aquaporin-2 water channel | AR (or AD rare) | 10% of congenital NDI |

**Exam pearl:** Lithium-NDI = most common acquired cause. May persist after lithium discontinuation (irreversible tubulointerstitial fibrosis). Amiloride blocks lithium entry via ENaC → can prevent/treat lithium-NDI.

### Diagnosis
1. Clinical polyuria + polydipsia
2. 24-hour urine: volume > 3 L, Osm < 300 mOsm/kg
3. **Water deprivation test:** urine remains dilute despite dehydration
4. **DDAVP challenge:** urine Osm does NOT concentrate (< 50% rise) → confirms nephrogenic
5. Partial NDI: urine Osm > plasma Osm but < 750-800 after DDAVP

### Water Deprivation Test Interpretation

| Result | Diagnosis |
|---|---|
| Urine concentrates normally (> 800) | Primary polydipsia |
| Urine remains dilute → concentrates after DDAVP (> 50% rise) | **Central DI (AVP-D)** |
| Urine remains dilute → NO response to DDAVP | **Nephrogenic DI (AVP-R)** |
| Partial concentration → further rise after DDAVP | Partial central DI |
| Partial concentration → no additional rise after DDAVP | Partial nephrogenic DI |

### Treatment

| Acquired NDI | Target underlying cause: stop lithium, correct hypercalcemia/hypokalemia |
|---|---|
| Congenital NDI | Symptomatic treatment (no cure) |
| All NDI | **Adequate fluid intake** (prevent dehydration), low-solute diet (↓ osmotic load) |
| Adjunctive | **Thiazide diuretics** (paradoxically reduce urine volume by 25-50%), **amiloride** (especially lithium-NDI), **NSAIDs** (↓ prostaglandin → ↑ AQP2 insertion) |

**Exam pearl:** Thiazide paradox in NDI: thiazide induces mild volume contraction → enhanced proximal Na⁺ and water reabsorption → less delivery to collecting duct → net decrease in urine volume. This is a CLASSIC exam question.

## High-Yield Exam Scenarios

1. **Postoperative hyponatremia in young woman:** High risk for ODS. Likely acute hyponatremia from perioperative fluids + pain/nausea-induced ADH. Treat with 3% NaCl if symptomatic. Correct slowly if > 48h.

2. **Na⁺ 118, chronic, asymptomatic, beer potomania:** Urine Osm < 100 (dilute). Low-solute intake limits water excretion capacity. Treatment: increase solute intake, gradual water restriction. Watch for rapid autocorrection when eating resumes.

3. **Elderly ICU patient, Na⁺ 158, intubated:** Inadequate free water replacement. Calculate water deficit. Replace with D5W over 48-72h. Target correction 10-12 mEq/L/day max.

4. **Post-transsphenoidal surgery, urine output 500 mL/h, Na⁺ 149:** Central DI (post-surgical). Give DDAVP. Monitor for triphasic response. Transient in most cases.

5. **Bipolar patient on lithium, polyuria 8 L/day, urine Osm 120:** Lithium-induced NDI. DDAVP will NOT work. Treatment: amiloride (blocks lithium entry), thiazide (paradoxical antidiuresis), low-solute diet, adequate water intake. Consider lithium discontinuation if psychiatrically feasible.

6. **SIAD vs. cerebral salt wasting (CSW):** Both have hyponatremia + high urine Na⁺ in neurosurgical patient. SIAD = euvolemic/slightly expanded. CSW = hypovolemic (negative fluid balance, weight loss, orthostasis). CSW treatment = NS (volume repletion); SIAD treatment = fluid restriction.

7. **Hyponatremia Na⁺ 122 corrected to 138 in 18 hours:** OVERCORRECTION (16 mEq/L in < 24h). Immediately: stop all active treatment, give DDAVP 2 mcg IV, start D5W infusion. Goal: re-lower Na⁺ back toward 130-132 to limit ODS risk.

8. **Thiazide-induced hyponatremia:** Most common drug-induced hyponatremia in elderly. Mechanism: impaired diluting segment (DCT) + continued ADH activity. Stop thiazide, NS if hypovolemic. Often recurs if rechallenged.
